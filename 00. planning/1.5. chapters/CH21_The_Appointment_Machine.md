# Chapter 21: The Appointment Machine
## Confirmation Battles as Constitutional Drama Across 230 Years

### Chapter Overview
This chapter synthesizes the full history of Supreme Court appointments to reveal how the confirmation process has become a primary mechanism for constructing and maintaining judicial supremacy. From Washington's quick Senate consents to Garland's 293-day blockade, the appointment process functions as constitutional drama that reveals deeper struggles over who decides constitutional meaning.

### Core Argument
The central transformation in Supreme Court appointments is not simply "it got worse" but rather "the visibility of the battle changed while the underlying fight over who decides remained constant." Confirmation battles serve as legitimacy temperature gauges, tracking the Court's authority across American history.

### Chapter Structure

#### Part I: The Early Republic (1789-1865)
- **Washington's model**: Quick consents, emphasis on qualifications over ideology
- **The Adams-Marbury transition**: First recognition that appointments shape constitutional interpretation
- **Jeffersonian resistance**: Impeachment as early appointment check
- **Jacksonian challenge**: Senate begins considering constitutional philosophy
- **Civil War appointments**: First explicitly ideological confirmations

#### Part II: The Gilded Age to New Deal (1865-1937)
- **Business court construction**: Corporate bar shapes appointments
- **Progressive resistance**: First organized ideological opposition
- **Court-packing crisis**: Appointment process becomes explicit constitutional battleground
- **The switch**: How appointment threats changed Court behavior

#### Part III: The Modern Era (1937-1987)
- **Post-New Deal consensus**: Merit-based appointments with ideological compatibility
- **Civil rights transformation**: Appointment process as civil rights battleground
- **Nixon's southern strategy**: Explicit ideological appointment criteria
- **Reagan revolution**: Federalist Society begins shaping nomination pipeline

#### Part IV: The Contemporary Wars (1987-Present)
- **Bork confirmation**: First modern ideological battle
- **Thomas confirmation**: Personal character as proxy for constitutional philosophy
- **Garland blockade**: Senate refusal to consider as constitutional innovation
- **Kavanaugh confirmation**: Personal attacks as appointment warfare
- **Recent confirmations**: Partisan lockstep as final stage

#### Part V: The Legitimacy Temperature Chart
- **Pre-1950 proxy system**: Editorial tone, congressional threats, state resolutions
- **Post-1950 polling framework**: Gallup confidence, partisan gaps, institutional trust
- **Pattern analysis**: How confirmation battles track legitimacy crises
- **Predictive value**: Appointment intensity as Court authority indicator

### Key Sources and Evidence
- **Historical confirmation records**: Senate debate transcripts, vote patterns
- **Contemporary media analysis**: Newspaper editorial coverage across eras
- **Public opinion data**: Gallup, Pew, ANES polling on Court legitimacy
- **Scholarly analysis**: Political science literature on appointment politics

### Integration with Book's Themes
- **Democracy vs. Supremacy**: Appointments as democratic check on judicial power
- **Legitimacy measurement**: Confirmation battles as legitimacy indicators
- **Backlash cycles**: Appointment process as backlash mechanism
- **Comparative context**: U.S. appointment warfare unique among democracies

### Chapter Significance
This chapter provides the longitudinal analysis missing from traditional treatments of Supreme Court appointments. By showing how appointment battles track legitimacy crises across 230 years, it reveals the confirmation process as a structural mechanism for managing democratic-judicial tensions rather than just partisan warfare.

### Connection to Part VI
- Establishes appointment process as first mechanism of judicial supremacy reproduction
- Provides historical foundation for understanding contemporary appointment wars
- Demonstrates how appointment battles both reflect and construct judicial authority
