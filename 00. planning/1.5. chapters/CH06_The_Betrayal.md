# Chapter 6: The Betrayal (1873–1896)

### Chapter Overview
This chapter examines the systematic dismantling of Reconstruction's constitutional promise through judicial supremacy. The Betrayal Court transforms the 14th Amendment from a shield for democracy into a weapon for hierarchy, establishing patterns of racial and economic subordination that would define American law for half a century.

### Core Argument
The Betrayal represents judicial supremacy's most destructive application. By interpreting Reconstruction amendments to protect white supremacy rather than challenge it, the Court shows how judicial power can enforce hierarchy as effectively as it can protect democracy. The betrayal is not just of Reconstruction but of constitutional possibility itself.

### Chapter Structure

#### Part I: The Context of Retreat
- **Political abandonment**: Northern retreat from Reconstruction commitment
- **Southern resistance**: Organized opposition to racial equality
- **Economic transformation**: Rise of corporate power and labor conflict
- **Judicial opportunity**: Court as agent of counter-revolution

#### Part II: The Anticlassification Revolution
- **The Civil Rights Cases (1883)**: Declaring Civil Rights Act unconstitutional
- **Legal doctrine**: "State action" limitation on 14th Amendment
- **The consequences**: Private discrimination becomes constitutionally protected
- **The irony**: Amendment designed to protect freedom becomes shield for oppression

#### Part III: Economic Power and Judicial Protection
- **Lochner era beginnings**: Substantive due process for economic liberty
- **Corporate personhood**: 14th Amendment protects business interests
- **Labor regulation**: Court strikes down worker protections
- **The pattern**: Economic power receives same protection as white supremacy

#### Part IV: The Systematic Logic
- **Formal equality**: Separate but equal becomes constitutional doctrine
- **Procedural barriers**: Voting rights undermined through technical requirements
- **Federalism as shield**: States' rights used to block federal intervention
- **The synthesis**: Racial and economic hierarchy mutually reinforcing

#### Part V: The Jim Crow Constitution
- **Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)**: Legalizing segregation
- **The doctrine**: Separate but equal as constitutional principle
- **The reality**: Constitutional protection for racial hierarchy
- **The betrayal complete**: Reconstruction promise officially destroyed

### Key Sources and Evidence
- **Court opinions**: Full text of major betrayal decisions
- **Legal scholarship**: Critical analysis of constitutional reasoning
- **African American newspapers**: Black responses to Court decisions
- **Congressional records**: Failed attempts to overturn Court decisions

### Integration with Book's Themes
- **Democracy vs. Supremacy**: Court uses supremacy to destroy democratic promise
- **Legitimacy measurement**: Growing acceptance of hierarchical order
- **Backlash cycle**: Reconstruction retreat → Judicial betrayal → Jim Crow equilibrium
- **Race lens**: Antisubordination vs. anticlassification through-line central

### Chapter Significance
This chapter demonstrates judicial supremacy's capacity to enforce hierarchy as effectively as it can protect democracy. The Betrayal shows that judicial power is inherently neutral — it can serve either democratic or anti-democratic ends depending on judicial will.

### Connection to Subsequent Chapters
- Establishes the racial hierarchy that Chapter 10's Brown Court must confront
- Creates the economic doctrine that Chapter 8's New Deal Court challenges
- Sets the constitutional baseline that Chapter 7's Gilded Court extends
