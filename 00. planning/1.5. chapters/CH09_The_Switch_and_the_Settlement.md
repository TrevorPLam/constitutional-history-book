# Chapter 9: The Switch and the Settlement (1937–1953)

### Chapter Overview
This chapter examines the constitutional revolution that resolved the New Deal crisis and established the modern relationship between judicial supremacy and democratic governance. The "Switch" represents the Court's strategic accommodation to democratic power, creating a new equilibrium that would define American politics for three decades.

### Core Argument
The Switch demonstrates judicial supremacy's adaptive capacity. Rather than risk destruction through political confrontation, the Court strategically redefines its role to accommodate democratic innovation while preserving core judicial authority. This settlement shows how judicial supremacy can evolve to survive democratic challenges.

### Chapter Structure

#### Part I: The Constitutional Crisis Peak
- **Court-packing defeat**: FDR's political loss but strategic victory
- **Public opinion**: Growing support for New Deal over Court opposition
- **Political pressure**: Elections, constitutional amendments, court reform proposals
- **The tipping point**: Court recognizes existential threat to judicial authority

#### Part II: The Switch Moment
- **West Coast Hotel v. Parrish (1937)**: The formal reversal
- **Owen Roberts' shift**: Strategic change in judicial philosophy
- **Legal reasoning**: New deference to legislative judgment
- **The significance**: Court voluntarily limits its own supremacy

#### Part III: The New Constitutional Order
- **Rational basis review**: Court defers to economic expertise
- **Political question doctrine**: Court avoids major policy conflicts
- **The settlement**: Democratic governance with judicial oversight
- **The balance**: Core judicial authority preserved but role redefined

#### Part IV: The Post-War Consensus
- **Warren Court beginnings**: Transition from restraint to activism
- **Civil rights emergence**: Court becomes leader of democratic change
- **The new equilibrium**: Judicial supremacy as rights protector rather than blocker
- **Public legitimacy**: Court regains and expands public support

#### Part V: The Legacy of Accommodation
- **The lesson learned**: Judicial supremacy requires political adaptation
- **The precedent**: Court can survive by accommodating democratic power
- **The cost**: Settlement requires redefining judicial role
- **The foundation**: New basis for Court-democracy relations

### Key Sources and Evidence
- **Court opinions**: Parrish and post-Switch decisions
- **Roberts' papers**: Evidence of strategic calculation
- **Political polling**: Public opinion shifts on Court legitimacy
- **Legal scholarship**: Analysis of the Switch's constitutional significance

### Integration with Book's Themes
- **Democracy vs. Supremacy**: Court chooses accommodation over confrontation
- **Legitimacy measurement**: Recovery of public trust through adaptation
- **Backlash cycle**: New Deal crisis → Switch → Post-war consensus
- **Comparative context**: American model moves closer to international norms

### Chapter Significance
This chapter shows judicial supremacy's survival strategy. By choosing strategic adaptation over political destruction, the Court demonstrates that judicial authority can persist by redefining its relationship to democratic power rather than insisting on absolute supremacy.

### Backlash Preview (Part III Opening)
The post-New Deal era reveals a new backlash pattern: democratic accommodation (the Switch) → judicial legitimacy recovery → rights expansion (Warren Court) → new equilibrium. This shows how judicial supremacy can maintain authority by serving democratic ends rather than opposing them.

### Connection to Subsequent Chapters
- Establishes the settlement that Chapter 10's Brown Court transforms
- Creates the constitutional basis that Chapter 11's Warren Revolution expands
- Sets the adaptation precedent that future courts would follow
