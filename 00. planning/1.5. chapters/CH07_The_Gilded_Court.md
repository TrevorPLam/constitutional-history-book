# Chapter 7: The Gilded Court (1897–1920)

### Chapter Overview
This chapter examines how the Supreme Court cemented corporate and economic power through judicial supremacy during the Gilded Age. The Court becomes the primary protector of business interests against democratic regulation, establishing doctrines that would define American capitalism and require constitutional crisis to overturn.

### Core Argument
The Gilded Court demonstrates judicial supremacy serving hierarchy rather than democracy. By using the 14th Amendment to protect corporate power while simultaneously allowing racial segregation, the Court shows how judicial authority can enforce multiple forms of subordination simultaneously, creating a constitutional order that serves economic and racial elites.

### Chapter Structure

#### Part I: The Gilded Age Context
- **Economic transformation**: Rise of corporate capitalism and industrial concentration
- **Political context**: Weak government, strong business influence
- **Social conflict**: Labor struggles, populist movements, progressive reform
- **Judicial role**: Court as guardian of economic order

#### Part II: Corporate Personhood and Power
- **Santa Clara County v. Southern Pacific (1886)**: Corporate personhood established
- **14th Amendment for corporations**: Equal protection becomes business shield
- **Legal consequences**: Corporations receive same constitutional protections as citizens
- **The revolution**: Judicial supremacy creates new constitutional actors

#### Part III: The Lochner Era
- **Lochner v. New York (1905)**: Freedom of contract as constitutional right
- **Substantive due process**: Judicial protection of economic liberty
- **Labor regulation struck down**: Minimum wage, working hours, union rights
- **The doctrine**: Court imposes economic theory on democratic decisions

#### Part IV: Racial Subordination Maintained
- **Cumming v. Richmond (1896)**: Plessy applied to education
- **Williams v. Mississippi (1898)**: Disenfranchisement upheld
- **Berea College v. Kentucky (1908)**: Racial education restrictions allowed
- **The pattern**: Economic and racial hierarchy mutually reinforced

#### Part V: The Progressive Resistance
- **Political backlash**: Progressive movement against Court's economic doctrines
- **Academic criticism**: Legal scholars challenge Lochner reasoning
- **State resistance**: Some states continue regulation despite Court opposition
- **The stage set**: Constitutional crisis becomes necessary for change

### Key Sources and Evidence
- **Court opinions**: Full text of major Gilded Age decisions
- **Legal scholarship**: Progressive criticism of Court doctrines
- **Business records**: Corporate use of constitutional protections
- **Progressive newspapers**: Criticism of Court's economic decisions

### Integration with Book's Themes
- **Democracy vs. Supremacy**: Court consistently chooses economic hierarchy over democratic regulation
- **Legitimacy measurement**: Growing progressive criticism but continued business support
- **Backlash cycle**: Corporate power entrenched → Progressive resistance → New Deal crisis
- **Economic power lens**: Central to understanding Court's decisions

### Chapter Significance
This chapter shows judicial supremacy's capacity to protect economic power as effectively as it can enforce racial hierarchy. The Gilded Court demonstrates how judicial authority can create constitutional order that serves multiple forms of elite power simultaneously.

### Connection to Subsequent Chapters
- Establishes the economic doctrine that Chapter 8's New Deal Court directly confronts
- Creates the corporate power that Chapter 14's Rehnquist Court revives
- Sets the constitutional baseline that Chapter 9's Switch overturns
