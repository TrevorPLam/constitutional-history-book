# Chapter 25: The Open Cycles
## Contemporary Institutional Stasis as Historical Pattern

### Chapter Overview
This chapter addresses a methodological challenge that has become a substantive thesis: the book's backlash cycle template requires a Counter-Reaction beat ("what actually changes 5–10 years later"), but contemporary cases don't yet have one. Rather than treating this as a narrative failure, this chapter formalizes it as a thesis about contemporary institutional stasis: backlash is now systematically suppressed by the very structures that once allowed it to function as democratic correction.

### Core Argument
The open cycles are not evidence of incomplete analysis but the book's most important contemporary claim. When the beneficiary of a supremacy claim wins the next election, when Congress is gridlocked, and when a 6-3 majority can block legislative fixes, backlash cycles are systematically suppressed. This creates institutional stasis that is historically unprecedented and constitutionally dangerous.

### Chapter Structure

#### Part I: The Backlash Cycle Template Revisited

- **Historical examples**: How the pattern worked in previous eras

#### Part II: The Suppression Mechanisms
- **Electoral capture**: When supremacy beneficiaries control political branches
  - Trump v. United States followed by Trump's 2024 victory
  - Supreme Court immunity claims protected by presidential power
- **Congressional gridlock**: When legislative correction becomes impossible
  - Shelby County's unfixed voting rights formula
  - Ethics legislation blocked by Court allies
- **Judicial self-protection**: When the Court can block its own reform
  - 6-3 majority veto power over constitutional amendments
  - Procedural barriers to legislative action

#### Part III: Case Studies of Open Cycles

##### The Dobbs Cycle (2022-Present)
- **Supremacy claim**: Overturning 50 years of abortion rights precedent
- **Backlash**: Massive public opposition, state-level protection efforts
- **Suppression**: Federal legislative response blocked by Senate filibuster and Court majority
- **Current status**: Institutional stasis with ongoing democratic deficit

##### The Immunity Cycle (2024-Present)
- **Supremacy claim**: Presidential immunity from judicial oversight
- **Backlash**: Constitutional scholars, public opinion polls, congressional hearings
- **Suppression**: Beneficiary wins election, Court controls enforcement
- **Current status**: Supremacy claim becomes permanent institutional feature

##### The Ethics Cycle (2023-Present)
- **Supremacy claim**: Judicial immunity from congressional oversight and ethics enforcement
- **Backlash**: ProPublica investigations, public outrage, legislative proposals
- **Suppression**: Congressional action blocked, Court claims self-regulating authority
- **Current status**: Ethics crisis becomes permanent feature of judicial governance
