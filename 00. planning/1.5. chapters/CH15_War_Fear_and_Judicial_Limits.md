# Chapter 15: War, Fear, and Judicial Limits (2000–2008)

### Chapter Overview
This chapter examines the Bush Court's response to 9/11 and the War on Terror, showing how judicial supremacy adapts to claims of executive power while expanding its own authority. The post-9/11 era represents the complex intersection of national security, executive power, and judicial authority in a new constitutional context.

### Core Argument
The War on Terror demonstrates judicial supremacy's ambivalent relationship with executive power. While the Court often defers to executive claims in national security, it simultaneously expands its own authority over other areas, showing how judicial power can both enable and limit executive action depending on political context.

### Chapter Structure

#### Part I: The 9/11 Constitutional Context
- **National crisis**: Terrorism creates new constitutional order
- **Executive power claims**: Bush administration's authority assertions
- **Judicial role**: Court as potential check on emergency power
- **The uncertainty**: New constitutional territory for all branches

#### Part II: Deference in National Security
- **Hamdi v. Rumsfeld (2004)**: Limited due process for enemy combatants
- **Padilla v. Hanft (2004)**: Executive power over citizen detention
- **Boumediene v. Bush (2008)**: Presidential power in foreign affairs
- **The pattern**: Judicial deference to executive in security matters

#### Part III: Judicial Expansion Elsewhere
- **Bush v. Gore (2000)**: Court intervenes in presidential election
- **Citizens United (2010)**: Corporate political speech rights expanded
- **District of Columbia v. Heller (2008)**: Individual gun rights established
- **The contrast**: Deference to executive, activism in other areas

#### Part IV: The New Judicial Balance
- **Roberts Court emergence**: Transition from Rehnquist to new era
- **Conservative-liberal coalitions**: Unpredictable voting patterns
- **National security consensus**: Cross-ideological deference to executive
- **The result**: Complex new equilibrium of judicial power

#### Part V: The Legacy of Fear
- **Constitutional transformation**: War on Terror changes institutional balance
- **Judicial authority**: Both enhanced and constrained by new context
- **Political implications**: Court as national security player
- **The foundation**: New patterns for future executive-judicial conflicts
