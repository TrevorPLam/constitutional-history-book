# Chapter 3: The Jacksonian Challenge (1829–1852)

## Overview

**Time Span:** 1829–1852 (23 years)

**One-Paragraph Description:**
Andrew Jackson's presidency is the first sustained challenge to judicial supremacy. In _Worcester v. Georgia_ (1832), the Marshall Court orders Georgia to release a missionary and recognize Cherokee sovereignty. Georgia ignores the order; Jackson declines to enforce it. Worcester remains imprisoned; the Cherokee are removed via the Trail of Tears. The Court's order is "stillborn." Jackson also vetoes the Bank recharter, explicitly rejecting the Court's reasoning in _McCulloch_—he argues each branch interprets the Constitution independently (departmentalism). The Taney Court that follows is deferential to states and Democratic political power. This chapter shows that judicial supremacy without executive enforcement is an empty claim. Legitimacy divides regionally: Whigs support the Court, Jacksonians see it as an aristocratic threat to democracy. The enforcement paradox is fully revealed.

## Driving Question

What happens when a popular president explicitly rejects the Court's authority?

## Lenses Deployed

- **Primary Lens (Democracy vs. Supremacy):** Jacksonian departmentalism vs. Marshall's nationalism.
- **Secondary Lens 1 (Legitimacy):** Regional split; partisan (Whig vs. Democrat) views of the Court.
- **Secondary Lens 2 (Race):** Cherokee removal as a fundamental failure of judicial supremacy to protect vulnerable minorities.
- **Supporting Lens (Federalism):** State defiance (Georgia) as a limit on federal judicial power.
