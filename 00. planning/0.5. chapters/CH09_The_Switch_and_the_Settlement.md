# Chapter 9: The Switch and the Settlement (1937–1953)

## Overview

**Time Span:** 1937–1953 (16 years)

**One-Paragraph Description:**
The post-1937 Court stops striking down economic regulation and begins the "double standard": extreme deference to legislatures on economic policy, but heightened scrutiny for civil liberties and rights of "discrete and insular minorities" (_Carolene Products_ footnote 4, 1938). The Court upholds New Deal programs and the wartime economy but defers catastrophically in _Korematsu v. United States_ (1944). _Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer_ (1952), by contrast, shows the Court asserting supremacy against executive power: Truman's steel mill seizure is struck down, and he complies. The New Deal settlement establishes a new paradigm for judicial power, and the Court's legitimacy is restored.

## Driving Question

What does the New Deal crisis actually settle, and what does it leave unresolved?

## Lenses Deployed

- **Primary Lens (Democracy vs. Supremacy):** New settlement: deference on economics, scrutiny on rights.
- **Secondary Lens 1 (Legitimacy):** Reservoir refills post-1937 crisis.
- **Secondary Lens 2 (Race):** (Implicit) The seed of racial protection in _Carolene Products_.
- **Supporting Lens (National Security):** _Korematsu_ as failure; _Youngstown_ as assertion.
- **Supporting Lens (Economic Power):** New Deal ends liberty-of-contract era.
