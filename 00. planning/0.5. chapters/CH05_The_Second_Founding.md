# Chapter 5: The Second Founding (1865–1877)

## Overview

**Time Span:** 1865–1877 (12 years)

**One-Paragraph Description:**
The Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments are the most transformative constitutional changes in American history, abolishing slavery, guaranteeing equal protection and due process, and protecting voting rights. For a brief window, congressional supremacy is ascendant: Congress passes ambitious civil rights legislation and even strips the Court's jurisdiction (_Ex parte McCardle_, 1868). The Court is subordinate to the Radical Republican project. Early decisions suggest the amendments could be read broadly to protect Black citizenship. But enforcement depends entirely on federal troops and funding. This chapter establishes what the Constitution _could have been_—an antisubordination charter—and sets up the betrayal that follows.

## Driving Question

Can constitutional amendments remake the nation, and who will enforce them?

## Lenses Deployed

- **Primary Lens (Democracy vs. Supremacy):** Congressional supremacy at its apex; the Court as subordinate.
- **Secondary Lens 1 (Legitimacy):** Southern white opposition vs. Black hope.
- **Secondary Lens 2 (Race):** The antisubordination moment: the state explicitly targeting structural racial hierarchy.
- **Supporting Lens (Federalism):** Reconstruction Amendments as federal supremacy over states.
