# Chapter 6: The Betrayal (1873–1896)

## Overview

**Time Span:** 1873–1896 (23 years)

**One-Paragraph Description:**
The Court systematically guts the Reconstruction Amendments in a series of devastating decisions. _Slaughter-House Cases_ (1873) reads the 14th Amendment narrowly; _Cruikshank_ (1876) allows white paramilitary violence; _The Civil Rights Cases_ (1883) strikes down the Civil Rights Act of 1875; _Plessy v. Ferguson_ (1896) constitutionalizes "separate but equal." Meanwhile, Congress defunds the enforcement apparatus. The Court's supremacy here serves white supremacy explicitly, overriding congressional attempts to protect Black citizenship. Legitimacy among Black Americans collapses. This is antisubordination replaced by anticlassification: formal race-neutrality becomes the tool of structural oppression.

## Driving Question

How does the Court dismantle a constitutional revolution?

## Lenses Deployed

- **Primary Lens (Democracy vs. Supremacy):** Court overrides Congress’s civil rights enforcement.
- **Secondary Lens 1 (Legitimacy):** Black legitimacy collapse; Southern white legitimacy restoration.
- **Secondary Lens 2 (Race):** Betrayal of Reconstruction; judicial supremacy serving white supremacy.
- **Supporting Lens (Federalism):** States reimposing racial caste with Court blessing.

## Gap Integration

- **Gap 7: Race as Constitutional Through-Line (Antisubordination vs. Anticlassification).**
  - Integrate: The shift to _anticlassification_ formalism in _Slaughter-House_, _Cruikshank_, and _Plessy_—using formal race-neutrality to mask structural hierarchy.
- **Gap 2: The Enforcement Paradox Across Eras.**
  - Integrate: The collapse of enforcement following the withdrawal of federal troops and defunding of the Freedmen's Bureau.
- **Gap 3: Pre-1950 Legitimacy Proxy Methodology.**
  - Integrate: Use regional divergence in editorials and Frederick Douglass’s public condemnation of the Court as legitimacy proxies.
