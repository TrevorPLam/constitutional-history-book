# Chapter 15: War, Fear, and Judicial Limits (2000–2008)

## Overview

**Time Span:** 2000–2008 (8 years)

**One-Paragraph Description:**
The 9/11 attacks trigger the War on Terror, and the Bush administration asserts vast detention powers. The Court initially defers but gradually asserts review: _Hamdi v. Rumsfeld_ (2004), _Rasul v. Bush_ (2004), and _Boumediene v. Bush_ (2008). These are strong assertions of supremacy against both the executive and Congress. However, enforcement depends on executive willingness to comply, and the administration finds workarounds. The enforcement paradox persists. Roberts and Alito are confirmed, beginning the Court's significant rightward shift.

## Driving Question

What happens to judicial supremacy when national security and executive power collide?

## Lenses Deployed

- **Primary Lens (Democracy vs. Supremacy):** _Youngstown_ framework; presidential power vs. judicial inquiry.
- **Secondary Lens 1 (Legitimacy):** Court gains credit for checking Bush, but results are mixed.
- **Supporting Lens (National Security):** Court asserts review but enforcement remains limited (Guantánamo).
- **Supporting Lens (Enforcement Paradox):** Executive controls the actual detention sites.

## Gap Integration

- **Gap 2: The Enforcement Paradox Across Eras.**
  - Integrate: _Boumediene_ (2008) and the reality that Guantánamo remained open despite the habeas ruling.
- **Gap 10: Jurisdiction Stripping as Democratic Check.**
  - Integrate: _Boumediene_ as a rare instance where the Court struck down a congressional jurisdiction strip (the Military Commissions Act).
- **Gap 5: The Shadow Docket as Governance.**
  - Integrate: The early shadow docket governance—immersion in national security injunctions.
