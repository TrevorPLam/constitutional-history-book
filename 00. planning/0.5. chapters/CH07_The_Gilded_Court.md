# Chapter 7: The Gilded Court (1897–1920)

## Overview

**Time Span:** 1897–1920 (23 years)

**One-Paragraph Description:**
The Lochner era: the Court strikes down roughly 200 state and federal labor laws, wielding "liberty of contract" as a shield for corporate power. _Lochner v. New York_ (1905) invalidates maximum-hours laws; labor injunctions crush strikes. The Court transforms the 14th Amendment into a tool for protecting corporations while ignoring racial subordination. Holmes's _Lochner_ dissent captures the critique: the Court is imposing a laissez-faire economic theory as law. Legitimacy divides by class: elites support the Court; Progressives and labor see it as captured by wealth. This is judicial supremacy exercised aggressively on behalf of economic power.

## Driving Question

Can the Court use supremacy to protect property rights against democratic regulation?

## Lenses Deployed

- **Primary Lens (Democracy vs. Supremacy):** Court blocks democratic economic regulation.
- **Secondary Lens 1 (Legitimacy):** Class divide; Progressive backlash.
- **Secondary Lens 2 (Race):** 14th Amendment inverted to protect corporations while ignoring Jim Crow.
- **Supporting Lens (Economic Power):** "Liberty of contract" serves capital; expansion of corporate personhood.
