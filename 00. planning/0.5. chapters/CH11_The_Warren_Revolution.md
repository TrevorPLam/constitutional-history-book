# Chapter 11: The Warren Revolution (1963–1969)

## Overview

**Time Span:** 1963–1969 (6 years)

**One-Paragraph Description:**
The Warren Court's final years are the apex of judicial liberalism: _Gideon v. Wainwright_ (1963), _Reynolds v. Sims_ (1964), _Miranda v. Arizona_ (1966), _Loving v. Virginia_ (1967), and _Griswold v. Connecticut_ (1965). The Court is aligned with the Great Society liberal coalition, and its legitimacy is high despite controversial rulings—because elite consensus (legal profession, media) supports it. But Nixon campaigns explicitly against the Warren Court in 1968, promising "law and order" justices. The conservative legal movement begins organizing in reaction to Warren. This chapter captures judicial supremacy at its most confident—and plants the backlash that will reshape American law for the next 50 years.

## Driving Question

Can the Court drive a rights revolution from the bench?

## Lenses Deployed

- **Primary Lens (Democracy vs. Supremacy):** Court as rights vanguard; majoritarian alignment.
- **Secondary Lens 1 (Legitimacy):** High legitimacy despite controversy; elite alignment.
- **Secondary Lens 2 (Race):** Criminal procedure as a racialized issue (_Miranda_, _Gideon_).
- **Supporting Lens (Rights Cycles):** Expansion phase reaches its apex.
- **Supporting Lens (Regime Politics):** Court aligned with Great Society coalition.

## Gap Integration
