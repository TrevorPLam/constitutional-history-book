# Chapter 12: Nixon's Court and the Roe Backlash (1969–1980)

## Overview

**Time Span:** 1969–1980 (11 years)

**One-Paragraph Description:**
Nixon appoints four justices to reverse Warren Court liberalism, but the results are mixed. The Burger Court surprises Nixon with _Roe v. Wade_ (1973), which becomes the most significant backlash-generating decision since _Brown_. It mobilizes evangelical Christians and helps create the Religious Right. Lewis Powell’s 1971 Memo to the U.S. Chamber of Commerce launches the corporate litigation infrastructure that will mature in later chapters. _Buckley v. Valeo_ (1976) plants the seed for _Citizens United_. The Burger Court accidentally ensures that judicial appointments will be the defining political battle for generations.

## Driving Question

Can a president remake the Court through appointments?

## Lenses Deployed

- **Primary Lens (Democracy vs. Supremacy):** _Roe_ asserts supremacy; triggers backlash.
- **Secondary Lens 1 (Legitimacy):** Polarization of the Court's support.
- **Secondary Lens 2 (Race):** (Implicit) Shift from civil rights to racial backlash (_Bakke_).
- **Supporting Lens (Rights Cycles):** Reproductive rights established; affirmative action constrained.
- **Supporting Lens (Economic Power):** Powell Memo; corporate mobilization begins.

## Backlash Cycle: Roe and the Religious Right

| Beat                 | Event                                           | Outcome                                          |
| :------------------- | :---------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------- |
| **Decision**         | Roe v. Wade (1973) creates a right to abortion. | Court creates right using privacy/due process.   |
| **Reaction**         | Evangelical and Religious Right mobilization.   | Single-issue voters and ideological referendums. |
| **Counter-Reaction** | 50 years of chipping away; Dobbs (2022).        | Legal movement ultimately succeeds in reversal.  |
