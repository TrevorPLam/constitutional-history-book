# Chapter 8: The Court Seizes the Agenda (1916–1937)

## Overview

**Time Span:** 1916–1937 (21 years)

**One-Paragraph Description:**
The 1925 Judges' Bill converts the Court from a passive tribunal into an active agenda-setter: certiorari becomes discretionary, and the Rule of Four allows a minority of justices to force cases onto the docket. The Court chooses to block New Deal legislation systematically. FDR wins the 1936 landslide and proposes the court-packing plan. The threat works: Justice Roberts "switches in time" (1937), and the Court begins upholding New Deal laws. The crisis reveals the Court's vulnerability—its supremacy depends on political tolerance. This is the closest the U.S. has come to fundamentally limiting judicial supremacy through structural reform since Reconstruction.

## Driving Question

What happens when the Court's supremacy collides with an electoral landslide and democratic emergency?

## Lenses Deployed

- **Primary Lens (Democracy vs. Supremacy):** Court vs. electoral mandate; democratic override threat.
- **Secondary Lens 1 (Legitimacy):** Crisis of confidence; polling begins to show collapse.
- **Secondary Lens 2 (Race):** (Implicit) The Court's conservative focus on property vs. the emerging New Deal coalition.
- **Supporting Lens (Economic Power):** Lochner's last stand.
- **Supporting Lens (Regime Politics):** Court blocks new regime until it is forced to submit.

## Backlash Cycle: Lochner and the New Deal

| Beat                 | Event                                                        | Outcome                                                            |
| :------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Decision**         | Court strikes down labor laws, New Deal programs (1905–1935) | Liberty of contract blocks democratic regulation.                  |
| **Reaction**         | FDR wins 1936 landslide; proposes court-packing (1937)       | Court's legitimacy plummets; Congress considers reform.            |
| **Counter-Reaction** | Roberts "switches in time"; Court upholds New Deal laws      | Judicial supremacy survives but is redefined as "double standard." |
