# Chapter 14: The Rehnquist Court's Federalism Revolution (1986–2000)

## Overview

**Time Span:** 1986–2000 (14 years)

**One-Paragraph Description:**
The Rehnquist Court revives federalism as a constraint on congressional power: _United States v. Lopez_ (1995), _United States v. Morrison_ (2000), and _City of Boerne v. Flores_ (1997). These are assertions of judicial supremacy against Congress. The amicus infrastructure is fully deployed by corporate and ideological interests. Then _Bush v. Gore_ (2000): the Court stops Florida's recount and effectively decides a presidential election. Legitimacy takes a visible hit; the Court crosses into territory it always avoided. The Rubicon is crossed.

## Driving Question

Can the Court impose limits on federal power while maintaining democratic legitimacy?

## Lenses Deployed

- **Primary Lens (Democracy vs. Supremacy):** Court polices federalism boundaries; _Bush v. Gore_ decides an election.
- **Secondary Lens 1 (Legitimacy):** _Bush v. Gore_ damages the "reservoir of goodwill."
- **Supporting Lens (Federalism):** The Rehnquist "Federalism Revolution."

- **Supporting Lens (Regime Politics):** Court aligned with conservative movement.
