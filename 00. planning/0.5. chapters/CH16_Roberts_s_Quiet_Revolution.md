# Chapter 16: Roberts's Quiet Revolution (2005–2013)

## Overview

**Time Span:** 2005–2013 (8 years)

**One-Paragraph Description:**
John Roberts tenure begins with public statements about "calling balls and strikes," but he proceeds to systematically advance a conservative agenda through procedural restraint and strategic opinion assignments. Landmark decisions include _Citizens United_ (2010), which allows unlimited corporate political spending, and _Shelby County v. Holder_ (2013), which guts the Voting Rights Act. However, Roberts also joins liberals in _NFIB v. Sebelius_ (2012) to uphold the ACA, a strategic move that preserves institutional legitimacy while narrowing Commerce Clause doctrine. Legitimacy begins a visible slide, and partisan gaps widen as the "quiet revolution" proceeds.

## Driving Question

Can a Chief Justice appear moderate while systematically advancing a conservative agenda?

## Lenses Deployed

- **Primary Lens (Democracy vs. Supremacy):** Roberts asserts supremacy while appearing restrained.
- **Secondary Lens 1 (Legitimacy):** Partisan divergence accelerates.
- **Secondary Lens 2 (Race):** _Shelby County_ and _Parents Involved_ dismantle civil rights protections.
- **Supporting Lens (Economic Power):** _Citizens United_; corporate speech.
- **Supporting Lens (Passive Virtues):** _NFIB_ as strategic institutional preservation.

## Backlash Cycle: Citizens United

| Beat                 | Event                                                                 | Outcome                                                  |
| :------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Decision**         | Citizens United (2010) allows unlimited corporate political spending. | Court strikes down campaign finance limits.              |
| **Reaction**         | Obama criticizes decision; reform groups mobilize.                    | Democracy reform becomes a progressive organizing issue. |
| **Counter-Reaction** | Super PAC spending explodes; stands legally.                          | Energizes reform movement but money remains in politics. |

## Backlash Cycle: Shelby County

| Beat                 | Event                                                       | Outcome                                                      |
| :------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Decision**         | Shelby County (2013) strikes down VRA preclearance formula. | Court guts the VRA enforcement mechanism.                    |
| **Reaction**         | Immediate state-level voting restrictions (e.g., Texas).    | Enforcement collapse; proliferation of voting barriers.      |
| **Counter-Reaction** | Multi-year struggle; electoral mobilization (2018-2020).    | VRA permanently weakened; Congress fails to fix the formula. |
