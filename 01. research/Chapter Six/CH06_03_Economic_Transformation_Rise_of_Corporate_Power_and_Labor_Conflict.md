# CH06: Economic Transformation: Rise of Corporate Power and Labor Conflict

## Overview
The post-Reconstruction period saw dramatic economic transformation with the rise of corporate power and labor conflict, creating new constitutional questions that would redirect judicial attention from racial to economic issues.

## Primary Sources
### Corporate Power Rise
- **Industrial Revolution**: Rapid growth of railroads, manufacturing, and finance.
- **Corporate Consolidation**: Formation of trusts and monopolies.
- **Economic Concentration**: Wealth and power increasingly concentrated in corporate hands.

### Labor Conflict
- **Worker Organization**: Rise of labor unions and strikes.
- **Industrial Violence**: Conflict between workers and corporate interests.
- **Economic Inequality**: Growing gap between capital and labor.

- **James McPherson**: In "Battle Cry of Freedom" (1988), McPherson emphasizes the "industrial revolution" changing American society.
- **David Blight**: In "Race and Reunion" (2001), Blight analyzes the "economic priorities" that displaced racial justice.

- **Michael F. Holt**: In "The Political Crisis of the 1850s" (1978), Holt examines the "corporate power rise" in the Gilded Age.
- **Steven Hahn**: In "A Nation Under Our Feet" (2003), Hahn analyzes the "economic transformation" affecting all social issues.
- **Heather Cox Richardson**: In "The Death of Reconstruction" (2001), Richardson emphasizes the "industrial priorities" reshaping politics.

### Labor Conflict
- **William Freehling**: In "The Road to Disunion" (1990), Freehling examines the "labor conflict" emerging from industrialization.
- **Kenneth M. Stampp**: In "The Era of Reconstruction" (1965), Stampp analyzes the "economic inequality" of the Gilded Age.
- **Avery Craven**: In "Reconstruction: The Ending of the Civil War" (1969), Craven discusses the "industrial violence" replacing racial conflict.

### Historical Significance
- **Don E. Fehrenbacher**: In "The Dred Scott Case" (1978), Fehrenbarger examines the "economic transformation" affecting constitutional issues.
- **Vincent C. Hopkins**: In "Dred Scott's Case" (1967), Hopkins analyzes the "corporate power rise" changing judicial focus.
