# CH04: Political Consequences: Decision Inflames Rather Than Resolves Conflict

## Overview
Rather than resolving the sectional conflict over slavery, the Dred Scott decision dramatically inflamed tensions and accelerated the nation's path toward civil war. The Court's attempt at political resolution backfired catastrophically.

## Primary Sources
### Political Inflammation
- **Northern Reaction**: Widespread outrage and increased anti-slavery activism.
- **Southern Response**: Emboldened pro-slavery advocates and demands.
- **Political Polarization**: Exacerbated sectional divisions and mistrust.

### Conflict Acceleration
- **Republican Rise**: Decision became rallying cry for new anti-slavery party.
- **Compromise Failure**: Eliminated possibility of political settlement.
- **War Preparation**: Increased sectional military and political preparations.

## Historian Analyses
### Political Inflammation
- **James McPherson**: In "Battle Cry of Freedom" (1988), McPherson argues the decision "inflamed sectional passions" and accelerated conflict.
- **David Potter**: In "The Impending Crisis" (1976), Potter emphasizes the decision's "explosive political consequences."
- **Eric Foner**: In "Free Soil, Free Labor, Free Men" (1970), Foner analyzes how the decision "intensified sectional conflict."

### Conflict Acceleration
- **Sean Wilentz**: In "The Rise of American Democracy" (2005), Wilentz argues the decision "accelerated the path to war."
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe examines the decision's role in "precipitating the crisis."
- **Michael F. Holt**: In "The Political Crisis of the 1850s" (1978), Holt analyzes the decision's "war-accelerating impact."

### Political Realignment
- **Don E. Fehrenbacher**: In "The Dred Scott Case" (1978), Fehrenbacher documents the "political realignment" caused by the decision.
- **Vincent C. Hopkins**: In "Dred Scott's Case" (1967), Hopkins examines the "political transformation" following the ruling.
- **Robert Remini**: In "Henry Clay" (1991), Remini discusses the "compromise destruction" caused by the decision.

### Sectional Polarization
- **William Freehling**: In "The Road to Disunion" (1990), Freehling argues the decision "deepened sectional polarization."
- **Kenneth M. Stampp**: In "The Causes of the Civil War" (1965), Stampp analyzes the decision's "polarizing effect."
- **Avery Craven**: In "The Coming of the Civil War" (1950), Craven discusses the "sectional division" exacerbated by the decision.
