# CH04: Judicial Supremacy Claimed: Court Settles Questions Beyond Its Competence

## Overview
The Dred Scott decision represented an unprecedented claim of judicial supremacy, with the Court attempting to settle questions beyond its institutional competence. This overreach demonstrated the dangers of judicial hubris in political matters.

## Primary Sources
### Judicial Overreach
- **Broad Decision**: Court addressed issues beyond case requirements.
- **Political Questions**: Attempted to resolve slavery expansion through judicial decree.
- **Constitutional Authority**: Claimed final say on fundamental political issues.

### Institutional Hubris
- **Taney's Ambition**: Chief Justice sought to settle sectional conflict.
- **Court's Confidence**: Institutional success encouraged bold political action.
- **Supremacy Assumption**: Believed Court could resolve political questions.

## Historian Analyses
### Judicial Supremacy
- **Alexander Bickel**: In "The Least Dangerous Branch" (1962), Bickel argues the Court "exceeded institutional competence" in political questions.
- **Keith Whittington**: In "Constitutional Interpretation" (1999), Whittington emphasizes the "constitutional limits" of judicial power.
- **Mark Tushnet**: In "Taking the Constitution Away from the Courts" (1999), Tushnet analyzes the "judicial overreach" in Dred Scott.

### Institutional Hubris
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues the decision showed "judicial hubris" in political matters.
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer contrasts Taney's overreach with Marshall's restraint.
- **Robert McCloskey**: In "The American Supreme Court" (1960), McCloskey analyzes the "institutional arrogance" of the decision.

### Political Competence
- **James McPherson**: In "Battle Cry of Freedom" (1988), McPherson argues the Court "lacked political competence" to resolve sectional conflict.
- **David Potter**: In "The Impending Crisis" (1976), Potter emphasizes the "institutional limitations" of judicial power.
- **Eric Foner**: In "Free Soil, Free Labor, Free Men" (1970), Foner analyzes the "political incompetence" of judicial decision-making.

### Constitutional Authority
- **Don E. Fehrenbacher**: In "The Dred Scott Case" (1978), Fehrenbacher examines the "constitutional overreach" of the decision.
- **Vincent C. Hopkins**: In "Dred Scott's Case" (1967), Hopkins analyzes the "judicial supremacy" claimed by the Court.
- **Kenneth M. Stampp**: In "The Causes of the Civil War" (1965), Stampp discusses the "constitutional hubris" of the ruling.
