# CH04: Immediate Backlash: Northern Outrage, Southern Celebration, National Division

## Overview
The Dred Scott decision triggered immediate and intense backlash, with Northern outrage, Southern celebration, and deepening national division. The ruling accelerated sectional polarization and made compromise increasingly impossible.

## Primary Sources
### Northern Outrage
- **Newspaper Condemnation**: Widespread criticism in Northern press.
- **Political Protests**: Mass meetings and demonstrations against decision.
- **Legal Community**: Northern lawyers and judges condemned the ruling.

### Southern Celebration
- **Enthusiastic Endorsement**: Southern newspapers praised the decision.
- **Political Victory**: Pro-slavery politicians celebrated judicial victory.
- **Sectional Pride**: South viewed decision as vindication of their position.

### National Division
- **Sectional Polarization**: Decision deepened North-South divisions.
- **Political Realignment**: Accelerated formation of Republican Party.
- **Compromise Failure**: Eliminated middle ground for political settlement.

## Historian Analyses
### Northern Reaction
- **James McPherson**: In "Battle Cry of Freedom" (1988), McPherson documents the "intense Northern outrage" and political mobilization.
- **David Potter**: In "The Impending Crisis" (1976), Potter emphasizes the "Northern rejection" of the decision.
- **Eric Foner**: In "Free Soil, Free Labor, Free Men" (1970), Foner analyzes the "Northern protest movement" against the ruling.

### Southern Response
- **William Freehling**: In "The Road to Disunion" (1990), Freehling examines the "Southern celebration" and overconfidence.
- **Kenneth M. Stampp**: In "The Causes of the Civil War" (1965), Stampp analyzes the "Southern triumphalism" following the decision.
- **Avery Craven**: In "The Coming of the Civil War" (1950), Craven discusses the "Southern victory" perception.

### National Division
- **Sean Wilentz**: In "The Rise of American Democracy" (2005), Wilentz emphasizes the "national polarization" caused by the decision.
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe examines the "sectional division" acceleration.
- **Michael F. Holt**: In "The Political Crisis of the 1850s" (1978), Holt analyzes the "compromise destruction" caused by the ruling.

### Political Consequences
- **Don E. Fehrenbacher**: In "The Dred Scott Case" (1978), Fehrenbacher documents the "political realignment" following the decision.
- **Vincent C. Hopkins**: In "Dred Scott's Case" (1967), Hopkins examines the "sectional political transformation."
- **Robert Remini**: In "Henry Clay" (1991), Remini discusses the "political system breakdown" caused by the decision.
