# CH04: Political Context: Growing Sectional Tension Over Slavery Expansion

## Overview
The 1850s witnessed intensifying sectional tensions over slavery expansion, creating a political crisis that tempted the Court to intervene. The nation's growing division provided the backdrop for the Court's fateful decision to resolve the slavery question.

## Primary Sources
### Sectional Conflict
- **Compromise of 1850**: Attempt to balance slave and free state interests.
- **Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854)**: Repealed Missouri Compromise, inflamed sectional conflict.
- **Bleeding Kansas**: Violent conflict over slavery's expansion into territories.

### Political Polarization
- **Rise of Republican Party**: Anti-slavery coalition formed in 1854.
- **Sectional Voting Patterns**: North-South political alignment hardened.
- **National Debate**: Slavery became dominant political issue.

## Historian Analyses
### Sectional Crisis
- **James McPherson**: In "Battle Cry of Freedom" (1988), McPherson argues the 1850s saw "irrepressible conflict" over slavery expansion.
- **David Potter**: In "The Impending Crisis" (1976), Potter emphasizes the "sectional polarization" of the 1850s.
- **Eric Foner**: In "Free Soil, Free Labor, Free Men" (1970), Foner analyzes the "political realignment" over slavery.

### Political Context
- **Sean Wilentz**: In "The Rise of American Democracy" (2005), Wilentz emphasizes the "sectional breakdown" of national politics.
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe describes the "political crisis" of the 1850s.
- **Michael F. Holt**: In "The Political Crisis of the 1850s" (1978), Holt analyzes the "collapse of the second party system."

### Court's Temptation
- **Don E. Fehrenbacher**: In "The Dred Scott Case" (1978), Fehrenbacher argues the Court faced "temptation to resolve" the political crisis.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White emphasizes the Court's "opportunity to settle" sectional conflict.
- **Avery Craven**: In "The Coming of the Civil War" (1950), Craven analyzes the Court's "intervention temptation."
