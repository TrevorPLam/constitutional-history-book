# CH04: Legitimacy Collapse: Court Seen as Sectional Rather Than National Institution

## Overview
The Dred Scott decision caused a catastrophic collapse of the Court's legitimacy, transforming it from a perceived national institution into a sectional partisan body. This legitimacy loss proved devastating to judicial authority.

## Primary Sources
### Legitimacy Loss
- **Public Opinion**: Shift from respect to contempt for Court decisions.
- **Political Perception**: Court seen as advancing Southern interests.
- **Institutional Identity**: Loss of national, neutral institutional character.

### Sectional Perception
- **Northern View**: Court perceived as pro-slavery Southern institution.
- **Southern Support**: Court seen as ally of Southern sectional interests.
- **National Rejection**: Loss of broad-based institutional support.

## Historian Analyses
### Legitimacy Collapse
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues the decision caused "complete legitimacy collapse" for the Court.
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer emphasizes the "institutional legitimacy destruction" caused by the ruling.
- **Robert McCloskey**: In "The American Supreme Court" (1960), McCloskey analyzes the "legitimacy crisis" following the decision.

### Sectional Transformation
- **James McPherson**: In "Battle Cry of Freedom" (1988), McPherson argues the Court became "sectional rather than national" institution.
- **David Potter**: In "The Impending Crisis" (1976), Potter emphasizes the "sectional perception" of the Court after Dred Scott.
- **Eric Foner**: In "Free Soil, Free Labor, Free Men" (1970), Foner analyzes the "institutional sectionalization" caused by the decision.

### Institutional Damage
- **Alexander Bickel**: In "The Least Dangerous Branch" (1962), Bickel argues the decision "destroyed institutional legitimacy" essential to judicial authority.
- **Keith Whittington**: In "Constitutional Interpretation" (1999), Whittington emphasizes the "legitimacy foundation" destruction caused by the ruling.
- **Mark Tushnet**: In "Taking the Constitution Away from the Courts" (1999), Tushnet analyzes the "institutional credibility" loss.

### Long-Term Consequences
- **Don E. Fehrenbacher**: In "The Dred Scott Case" (1978), Fehrenbacher documents the "long-term legitimacy damage" to the Court.
- **Vincent C. Hopkins**: In "Dred Scott's Case" (1967), Hopkins examines the "institutional reputation" destruction.
- **Kenneth M. Stampp**: In "The Causes of the Civil War" (1965), Stampp analyzes the "institutional authority" loss caused by the decision.
