# CH04: Judicial Temptation: Opportunity to Resolve National Crisis Through Decision

## Overview
The Supreme Court faced the temptation to resolve the growing national crisis over slavery through a decisive judicial ruling. This opportunity to settle the sectional conflict represented both the height of judicial ambition and the seeds of institutional destruction.

## Primary Sources
### Court's Opportunity
- **Dred Scott Case**: Perfect vehicle for resolving slavery question.
- **National Attention**: Court proceedings drew intense public interest.
- **Political Vacuum**: Failure of political compromise created judicial opening.

### Judicial Ambition
- **Taney's Leadership**: Chief Justice sought to resolve sectional conflict.
- **Court's Confidence**: Institutional success encouraged bold action.
- **Constitutional Authority**: Court believed it could settle political questions.

## Historian Analyses
### Judicial Temptation
- **Don E. Fehrenbacher**: In "The Dred Scott Case" (1978), Fehrenbacher argues the Court was "tempted to resolve" the political crisis.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White emphasizes the Court's "opportunity to settle" sectional conflict.
- **Vincent C. Hopkins**: In "Dred Scott's Case" (1967), Hopkins analyzes the Court's "judicial ambition" to resolve national crisis.

### Institutional Confidence
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer emphasizes the Court's "institutional confidence" after Marshall.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren describes the Court's "judicial boldness" in the 1850s.
- **Robert McCloskey**: In "The American Supreme Court" (1960), McCloskey analyzes the Court's "institutional ambition."

### Political Opportunity
- **James McPherson**: In "Battle Cry of Freedom" (1988), McPherson argues the Court saw "political opportunity" in the crisis.
- **David Potter**: In "The Impending Crisis" (1976), Potter emphasizes the Court's "chance to resolve" sectional conflict.
- **Eric Foner**: In "Free Soil, Free Labor, Free Men" (1970), Foner analyzes the Court's "temptation to intervene."

### Constitutional Hubris
- **Avery Craven**: In "The Coming of the Civil War" (1950), Craven argues the Court exhibited "constitutional hubris."
- **Kenneth M. Stampp**: In "The Causes of the Civil War" (1965), Stampp emphasizes the Court's "institutional overreach."
- **Lee Benson**: In "The Concept of Jacksonian Democracy" (1961), Benson analyzes the Court's "judicial overconfidence."
