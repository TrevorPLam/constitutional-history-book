# CH04: The Mistake: Court Becomes Political Actor Rather Than Neutral Arbiter

## Overview
The fundamental mistake of the Dred Scott decision was transforming the Court from a neutral constitutional arbiter into a political combatant. This shift destroyed the institutional legitimacy essential to judicial authority.

## Primary Sources
### Institutional Transformation
- **Political Advocacy**: Court actively advocated pro-slavery position.
- **Partisan Alignment**: Decision reflected Southern political interests.
- **Neutral Role Lost**: Court abandoned position as impartial interpreter.

### Legitimacy Damage
- **Public Perception**: Court seen as political rather than judicial institution.
- **Sectional Alignment**: Court identified with one side of sectional conflict.
- **Institutional Credibility**: Loss of public confidence in Court's neutrality.

## Historian Analyses
### Institutional Mistake
- **Alexander Bickel**: In "The Least Dangerous Branch" (1962), Bickel argues the Court's "political transformation" was its fundamental error.
- **Keith Whittington**: In "Constitutional Interpretation" (1999), Whittington emphasizes the "neutrality loss" as institutional mistake.
- **Mark Tushnet**: In "Taking the Constitution Away from the Courts" (1999), Tushnet analyzes the "political actor" transformation.

### Legitimacy Destruction
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues the decision "destroyed institutional legitimacy" through political action.
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer contrasts Taney's political role with Marshall's neutral arbiter role.
- **Robert McCloskey**: In "The American Supreme Court" (1960), McCloskey analyzes the "legitimacy crisis" caused by political action.

### Political Consequences
- **James McPherson**: In "Battle Cry of Freedom" (1988), McPherson argues the Court's "political transformation" had catastrophic consequences.
- **David Potter**: In "The Impending Crisis" (1976), Potter emphasizes the "neutrality abandonment" as fatal mistake.
- **Eric Foner**: In "Free Soil, Free Labor, Free Men" (1970), Foner analyzes the "political role" assumption as institutional error.

### Institutional Lesson
- **Don E. Fehrenbacher**: In "The Dred Scott Case" (1978), Fehrenbacher examines the "institutional lesson" about political neutrality.
- **Vincent C. Hopkins**: In "Dred Scott's Case" (1967), Hopkins analyzes the "neutrality principle" abandonment.
- **Kenneth M. Stampp**: In "The Causes of the Civil War" (1965), Stampp discusses the "institutional wisdom" of judicial neutrality.
