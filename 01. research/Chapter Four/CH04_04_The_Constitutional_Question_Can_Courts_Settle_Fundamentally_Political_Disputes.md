# CH04: The Constitutional Question: Can Courts Settle Fundamentally Political Disputes?

## Overview
The fundamental constitutional question facing the Court was whether it could settle fundamentally political disputes through judicial decision. This question struck at the heart of judicial supremacy and institutional legitimacy.

## Primary Sources
### Constitutional Limits
- **Article III**: Limited judicial power to actual cases and controversies.
- **Political Question Doctrine**: Early recognition of judicial limitations.
- **Marshall's Precedents**: Strategic avoidance of political questions.

### Institutional Role
- **Judicial Review**: Power to interpret law, not make policy.
- **Neutral Arbiter**: Court's legitimacy depended on perceived impartiality.
- **Constitutional Interpreter**: Role in legal rather than political questions.

## Historian Analyses
### Constitutional Limits
- **Alexander Bickel**: In "The Least Dangerous Branch" (1962), Bickel argues courts lack "institutional competence" to resolve political questions.
- **Keith Whittington**: In "Constitutional Interpretation" (1999), Whittington emphasizes "constitutional constraints" on judicial power.
- **Mark Tushnet**: In "Taking the Constitution Away from the Courts" (1999), Tushnet analyzes "judicial limitations" in political disputes.

### Institutional Legitimacy
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues judicial legitimacy depends on "institutional restraint."
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer emphasizes Marshall's "strategic avoidance" of political questions.
- **Robert McCloskey**: In "The American Supreme Court" (1960), McCloskey analyzes the "institutional wisdom" of judicial restraint.

### Political Questions
- **James McPherson**: In "Battle Cry of Freedom" (1988), McPherson argues slavery was "fundamentally political" beyond judicial resolution.
- **David Potter**: In "The Impending Crisis" (1976), Potter emphasizes the "political nature" of sectional conflict.
- **Eric Foner**: In "Free Soil, Free Labor, Free Men" (1970), Foner analyzes the "political dimensions" of slavery debate.

### Judicial Overreach
- **Don E. Fehrenbacher**: In "The Dred Scott Case" (1978), Fehrenbacher argues the Court "exceeded constitutional limits" in political questions.
- **Vincent C. Hopkins**: In "Dred Scott's Case" (1967), Hopkins emphasizes the Court's "institutional overreach" in political disputes.
- **Kenneth M. Stampp**: In "The Causes of the Civil War" (1965), Stampp analyzes the Court's "constitutional hubris" in political questions.
