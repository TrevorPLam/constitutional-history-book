# CH05: United States v. Cruikshank (1876): Undermining Federal Civil Rights Protection

## Overview
United States v. Cruikshank (1876) severely undermined federal civil rights protection by restricting federal authority to prosecute private conspiracies violating civil rights, effectively leaving Black citizens vulnerable to white supremacist violence.

## Primary Sources
### The Decision
- **United States v. Cruikshank**: 92 U.S. 542 (1876) - limited federal civil rights enforcement.
- **Private Action Doctrine**: Court held federal government could not regulate private discrimination.
- **State Action Requirement**: Federal protection only applied to state action, not private violence.

### Legal Consequences
- **Federal Authority Limited**: Decision restricted federal power to protect civil rights.
- **State Responsibility**: Court left civil rights protection to states, many of which were hostile.
- **Violence Impunity**: Decision effectively immunized private racial violence from federal prosecution.
