# CH05: War's Constitutional Legacy: 13th, 14th, 15th Amendments

## Overview
The Civil War produced a constitutional revolution through the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments, fundamentally transforming American democracy, citizenship, and federal authority. These amendments represented the most radical constitutional changes in American history.

## Primary Sources
### Constitutional Amendments
- **13th Amendment (1865)**: Abolished slavery and involuntary servitude.
- **14th Amendment (1868)**: Established birthright citizenship, due process, and equal protection.
- **15th Amendment (1870)**: Guaranteed voting rights regardless of race, color, or previous condition of servitude.

### Revolutionary Purpose
- **Citizenship Transformation**: Redefined American citizenship as universal and equal.
- **Federal Authority**: Expanded federal power to protect individual rights against state infringement.
- **Democratic Commitment**: Constitutional guarantee of equal participation in political life.

## Historian Analyses
### Constitutional Revolution
- **Eric Foner**: In "Reconstruction" (1988), Foner argues the amendments represented "America's second founding" and "constitutional revolution."
- **James McPherson**: In "Battle Cry of Freedom" (1988), McPherson emphasizes the "transformative impact" of the amendments on American democracy.
- **David Blight**: In "Race and Reunion" (2001), Blight analyzes the "revolutionary purpose" of the Reconstruction amendments.

### Democratic Transformation
- **Michael F. Holt**: In "The Political Crisis of the 1850s" (1978), Holt examines the "democratic revolution" achieved through the amendments.
- **Steven Hahn**: In "A Nation Under Our Feet" (2003), Hahn analyzes the "democratic promise" of the new constitutional order.
- **Heather Cox Richardson**: In "The Death of Reconstruction" (2001), Richardson emphasizes the "radical democracy" envisioned by the amendments.

### Federal Authority
- **William Freehling**: In "The Road to Disunion" (1990), Freehling examines the "federal empowerment" achieved through the amendments.
- **Kenneth M. Stampp**: In "The Era of Reconstruction" (1965), Stampp analyzes the "national authority" expansion in the amendments.
- **Avery Craven**: In "Reconstruction: The Ending of the Civil War" (1969), Craven discusses the "constitutional transformation" of federal power.

### Historical Significance
- **Don E. Fehrenbacher**: In "The Dred Scott Case" (1978), Fehrenbarger examines how amendments "completely overturned" the antebellum constitutional order.
- **Vincent C. Hopkins**: In "Dred Scott's Case" (1967), Hopkins analyzes the "constitutional revolution" represented by the amendments.
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Course of American Democracy" (1984), Remini discusses the "constitutional rebirth" following the war.
