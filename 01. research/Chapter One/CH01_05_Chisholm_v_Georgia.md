# CH01: Chisholm v. Georgia (1793)

## Overview
Chisholm v. Georgia (1793) was the Supreme Court's first major assertion of federal judicial authority over states, ruling that states could be sued by citizens of other states under Article III. The decision provoked intense backlash, exposing the Court's vulnerability without political support, and led to the 11th Amendment.

## Primary Sources
### Case Facts
- **Background**: During the Revolutionary War, Georgia commissioners purchased supplies from South Carolina merchant Robert Farquhar but failed to pay. After Farquhar's death, executor Alexander Chisholm sued Georgia in state court (denied) and federal circuit court (dismissed on immunity grounds).
- **Supreme Court Appeal**: Chisholm appealed to the Supreme Court. Georgia refused to appear, arguing sovereign immunity and lack of jurisdiction.

### Supreme Court Opinion
- **Decision**: 4-1 in favor of Chisholm (Justices Blair, Cushing, Wilson, Jay; Iredell dissenting).
- **Chief Justice John Jay's Opinion**: Held that Article III grants federal courts jurisdiction over suits "between a State and Citizens of another State." States are not immune from such suits; sovereignty does not bar private citizens from seeking redress.
- **Key Reasoning**: The Constitution treats states as legal entities amenable to federal jurisdiction, unlike absolute monarchs. Dismissing immunity claims, Jay argued the Court's duty was to interpret the Constitution as written.
- **Dissent (Justice James Iredell)**: Argued for state sovereign immunity based on common law and the Constitution's structure, warning that allowing suits against states would undermine federalism.

### Aftermath
- **Political Reaction**: The decision outraged states' rights advocates. Newspapers and politicians denounced it as judicial overreach. Virginia and Kentucky passed resolutions questioning federal authority.
- **11th Amendment (1795)**: Ratified in response, it barred federal courts from hearing suits against states by citizens of other states or foreign countries, effectively overturning Chisholm.

## Historian Analyses
### Assertion of Federal Power
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes Chisholm as the Court's first bold claim of supremacy, but one that "backfired politically." It demonstrated judicial authority but highlighted the need for restraint.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren notes the decision's "startling novelty," as no state had been sued in federal court before. It tested the limits of Article III without prior consensus.

### Legitimacy and Backlash
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Chisholm revealed the Court's fragility. The backlash showed that judicial power required political acquiescence; without it, assertions risked reversal.
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell views Chisholm as exemplifying Anti-Federalist fears of federal tyranny. The 11th Amendment was a direct rebuke, limiting the Court's jurisdiction and affirming state sovereignty.

### Broader Implications
- **Federalism Debate**: Historians like Jack Rakove ("Original Meanings," 1996) see it as clarifying Article III but exposing tensions between national and state power.
- **Precedent for Judicial Review**: Though not striking down a law, Chisholm asserted federal judicial supremacy, setting a stage for Marbury v. Madison.
