# CH01: The Constitution's Judicial Vacuum

## Overview
The U.S. Constitution does not explicitly grant federal courts the power of judicial review—the authority to declare laws unconstitutional. This absence creates what historians term a "judicial vacuum," where the judiciary's role in enforcing constitutional limits is implied rather than stated, leading to ambiguity about the Court's authority in the early republic.

## Primary Sources
### Constitutional Text
- **Article III**: Vests judicial power in federal courts but does not mention invalidating laws. It gives jurisdiction over "all cases, in law and equity, arising under this Constitution."
- **Article VI (Supremacy Clause)**: Declares the Constitution as supreme law, requiring judges to follow it over conflicting statutes, but does not explicitly authorize courts to strike down laws.
- **Comparison to State Constitutions**: Many state constitutions (e.g., Massachusetts 1780) explicitly provided for judicial review, highlighting the federal omission.

### Constitutional Convention Debates
- **Madison's Notes**: James Madison recorded discussions where delegates assumed courts could declare laws "null & void" if unconstitutional (June 18, 1787).
- **Delegate Comments**:
  - Elbridge Gerry: Noted judges could set aside laws as unconstitutional, citing state practices.
  - Luther Martin: Argued judges already had a "negative on the laws" through judicial review.
  - George Mason: Stated judges "could declare an unconstitutional law void," though limited to plainly unconstitutional ones.
- **Council of Revision Proposal**: Alexander Hamilton's plan for a council including judges to veto laws was rejected, but objections assumed judicial review existed separately.

### Federalist Papers
- **Federalist No. 78 (Hamilton)**: Argues for judicial review as necessary to protect constitutional limits, but presents it as a theoretical necessity rather than constitutional text.

## Historian Analyses
### Scholarly Consensus on Absence
- **Implied Power**: Historians like Akhil Amar and Laurence Tribe argue judicial review is implied from Article III's jurisdiction and Article VI's supremacy. Courts must interpret the Constitution to decide cases, inherently including invalidating conflicting laws.
- **Not Explicit**: Unlike European systems or some state models, the federal Constitution lacks a clause like "the judiciary shall have power to declare laws void."

### Delegate Views at Convention
- **Support for Judicial Review**: Scholars count 15-40 delegates expressing support during debates. For example:
  - William Treanor: "Judicial Review Before Marbury" (2005) finds widespread assumption of the power among framers.
  - Research by constitutional historians: At least 25 of 55 delegates indicated support, with only 3-6 opposed.
- **Opposition**: A minority, including possibly Mercer and Dickinson, expressed doubts, but no majority rejection.
- **Silence as Acquiescence**: The absence of controverting statements suggests general acceptance, though not unanimous endorsement.

### Historical Context
- **State Precedents**: Before 1789, several states practiced judicial review (e.g., New Jersey 1777 cases).
- **English Common Law**: American framers drew from Blackstone, who denied parliamentary supremacy over fundamental law, implying judicial checks.
- **Ambiguity's Purpose**: Historians like Charles Warren argue the silence was intentional to avoid controversy, allowing gradual development.

## Implications for Early Court
The vacuum necessitated the Court to assert authority through practice rather than text, setting the stage for Marbury v. Madison's establishment of judicial review as precedent.
