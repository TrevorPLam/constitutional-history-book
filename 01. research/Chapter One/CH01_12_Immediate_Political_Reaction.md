# CH01: Immediate Political Reaction

## Overview
The immediate political reaction to Marbury v. Madison (1803) was a Federalist defeat and Republican victory: Jefferson's administration blocked the commissions, and Republicans consolidated power. However, this short-term loss laid the groundwork for the Court's long-term institutional gain through judicial review.

## Primary Sources
### Jefferson's Response
- **Refusal to Deliver Commissions**: Jefferson instructed Madison not to deliver the commissions, denying Marbury and others their positions. This thwarted Adams's "midnight appointments."
- **Correspondence**: Jefferson wrote to Levi Lincoln (1803) dismissing the decision as "a very dangerous doctrine" but acknowledged it had "no effect" on his administration.

### Election of 1804
- **Federalist Decline**: Marbury emboldened Republicans. Jefferson defeated Federalist Charles Pinckney in a landslide, securing Republican dominance.
- **Congressional Actions**: Republicans passed the 12th Amendment (1804) to reform presidential elections, further weakening Federalists.

### Newspaper and Public Reactions
- **Republican Press**: Papers like the Richmond Enquirer hailed Jefferson's victory, calling the Court "impotent."
- **Federalist Press**: The Gazette of the United States criticized the decision as "usurpation" but lamented the political loss.

## Historian Analyses
### Federalist Defeat, Republican Victory
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer describes the reaction as a "political rout" for Federalists. Marbury failed to protect their appointees, accelerating their decline.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White notes Republicans' triumph: the Court could assert review but not enforce it, leaving Jefferson unchallenged.

### Paradox of Defeat and Gain
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson argues the immediate defeat masked long-term victory. Political failure established precedent without crisis.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren sees the reaction as teaching that judicial power grows from political setbacks, not victories.

### Institutional Implications
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell views the reaction as confirming Anti-Federalist fears but proving judicial resilience. Federalist loss enabled Court independence.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove describes it as a "strategic withdrawal" where political defeat created institutional triumph.
