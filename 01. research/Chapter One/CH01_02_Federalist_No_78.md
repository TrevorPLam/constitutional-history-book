# CH01: Federalist No. 78

## Overview
Federalist No. 78, written by Alexander Hamilton on May 28, 1788, is a key essay in The Federalist Papers defending the structure of the federal judiciary under the proposed Constitution. It argues for an independent judiciary as a check on legislative power and implies the courts' role in upholding constitutional limits, though it does not explicitly state that courts can invalidate laws.

## Primary Source: Full Text Summary
Hamilton discusses the judiciary's design, emphasizing:
- **Appointment and Tenure**: Judges appointed by the executive with Senate advice, serving during good behavior for independence.
- **Judiciary as Weakest Branch**: "The judiciary... has no influence over either the sword or the purse; no direction either of the strength or of the wealth of the society; and can take no active resolution whatever. It may truly be said to have neither FORCE nor WILL, but merely judgment."
- **Role as Constitutional Guardian**: "The courts were designed to be an intermediate body between the people and the legislature... to keep the latter within the limits assigned to their authority."
- **Interpretation Duty**: "The interpretation of the laws is the proper and peculiar province of the courts. A constitution is, in fact, and must be regarded by the judges, as a fundamental law."
- **Preference for Constitution**: "If there should happen to be an irreconcilable variance between the two [Constitution and statute], that which has the superior obligation and validity ought, of course, to be preferred; or, in other words, the Constitution ought to be preferred to the statute."

The essay concludes that the judiciary's permanence and limited powers make it suitable for checking legislative encroachments on constitutional boundaries.

## Historian Analyses
### Standard Interpretation: Support for Judicial Review
- **Hamilton's Intent**: Most constitutional scholars, including Clinton Rossiter and Charles Kesler, argue Federalist No. 78 lays the intellectual groundwork for judicial review. Hamilton's emphasis on courts interpreting the Constitution and preferring it over statutes implies the power to declare laws void.
- **Influence on Marbury**: Chief Justice John Marshall cited Federalist No. 78 in Marbury v. Madison (1803) to justify judicial review, interpreting Hamilton's words as endorsing the practice.
- **Modern Views**: Historians like Jack Rakove and Akhil Amar see it as evidence that framers expected courts to enforce constitutional limits, filling the "judicial vacuum" in the Constitution.

### Revisionist Interpretation: Legislative Supremacy
- **Gary Wills' Argument**: In "Explaining America: The Federalist" (1981), Wills contends Hamilton advocated for legislative supremacy, not judicial nullification. The judiciary would "guide" the legislature through interpretation, but laws could only be changed by new legislation, not judicial decree. Wills points to Hamilton's later actions (e.g., in the Bank of the United States case) as inconsistent with strong judicial review.
- **Context of Federalism**: Wills argues Hamilton viewed the Constitution as a flexible framework, with courts advising rather than overriding elected branches. The "preference" for the Constitution means legislatures would self-correct upon judicial guidance.
- **Critique**: Other historians, like Saul Cornell, dispute Wills, noting Hamilton's language aligns with common law traditions of judicial supremacy over acts of Parliament.

### Broader Context
- **Anti-Federalist Response**: Brutus (Robert Yates) criticized Federalist No. 78 in essays opposing the Constitution, warning that an independent judiciary could become a "despotic aristocracy" unchecked by the people.
- **Historical Impact**: The essay influenced ratification debates, reassuring Federalists that the judiciary would protect minority rights and constitutional boundaries against majority tyranny.
