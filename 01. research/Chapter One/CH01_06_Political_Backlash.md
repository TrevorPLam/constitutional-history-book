# CH01: Political Backlash

## Overview
The political backlash to Chisholm v. Georgia (1793) demonstrated the limits of judicial authority in the early republic. States and Anti-Federalists viewed the decision as an overreach threatening state sovereignty, leading to the 11th Amendment and underscoring that judicial claims require political acceptance to be effective.

## Primary Sources
### Immediate Reactions
- **State Responses**: Georgia and other states denounced the decision. Georgia's governor refused to comply, arguing the Court lacked jurisdiction. Virginia and South Carolina legislatures condemned it as unconstitutional.
- **Congressional Debate**: In 1794, Congress debated restricting the Court's jurisdiction. Federalists defended the decision, but Anti-Federalists pushed for limits on suits against states.
- **Newspaper Coverage**: Federalist papers (e.g., Gazette of the United States) supported the ruling as upholding federal supremacy. Republican papers (e.g., National Gazette) attacked it as aristocratic tyranny, echoing Patrick Henry's ratification concerns.

### 11th Amendment (1795)
- **Text**: "The Judicial power of the United States shall not be construed to extend to any suit in law or equity, commenced or prosecuted against one of the United States by Citizens of another State, or by Citizens or Subjects of any Foreign State."
- **Ratification**: Proposed by Congress in 1794, ratified in 1795. It overturned Chisholm by barring federal courts from hearing suits against states by out-of-state citizens.
- **Purpose**: A direct congressional check on judicial authority, affirming state sovereign immunity and limiting federal judicial power over states.

### Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions (1798)
- **Context**: While primarily responding to the Alien and Sedition Acts, these resolutions (authored by Jefferson and Madison) articulated states' rights principles that echoed Chisholm backlash.
- **Virginia Resolution**: Argued states could nullify unconstitutional federal laws, implicitly questioning federal judicial supremacy.
- **Impact**: Reinforced the political limits on judicial power, showing elected branches could curb the Court.

## Historian Analyses
### Congressional Check on Judicial Authority
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren calls the 11th Amendment a "rebuke" to the Court, proving judicial claims must align with political will. Without congressional support, decisions like Chisholm risked reversal.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues the backlash established a "dialogue" between branches. The Court could assert power, but politics could retract it, creating equilibrium.

### Lesson on Political Acceptance
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer notes Chisholm's lesson: judicial authority is contingent. Marshall learned to avoid direct confrontation, building power through precedent rather than fiat.
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees the backlash as vindicating Anti-Federalist warnings. The 11th Amendment protected state sovereignty, limiting the Court's role in federalism disputes.

### Broader Implications
- **Judicial Restraint**: Historians like Jack Rakove ("Original Meanings," 1996) argue the episode taught the Court prudence. Assertions without political backing invited backlash, shaping the judiciary's cautious approach.
- **Evolution of Federalism**: The backlash initiated cycles of judicial expansion followed by political retrenchment, a pattern recurring in American constitutional history.
