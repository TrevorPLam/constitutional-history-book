# CH01: Foundation for Supremacy

## Overview
Marbury v. Madison (1803) laid the foundation for judicial supremacy by establishing judicial review as the mechanism for courts to claim final authority over constitutional interpretation. This precedent enabled future assertions of supremacy, allowing the Court to invalidate laws and shape policy without direct political confrontation.

## Primary Sources
### Marbury's Doctrine
- **Judicial Review Established**: Marshall's opinion declared courts' duty to interpret the Constitution, invalidating conflicting laws. This created the tool for supremacy claims.
- **Future Applications**:
  - **Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857)**: Court claimed supremacy over Congress on slavery, using Marbury's precedent.
  - **Roe v. Wade (1973)**: Asserted supremacy over state laws on abortion, rooted in Marbury.
  - **Bush v. Gore (2000)**: Court intervened in election, citing ultimate interpretive authority.

### Institutional Legacy
- **Court as Final Arbiter**: Post-Marbury, the Court positioned itself as the "supreme interpreter," with review used to override elected branches.
- **Constitutional Supremacy**: Reinforced that the Constitution, as interpreted by courts, trumps legislation.

## Historian Analyses
### Enabling Future Claims
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Marbury provided the "intellectual foundation" for supremacy, allowing courts to challenge majoritarian decisions.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes it as the cornerstone, enabling the Court to evolve from weak to dominant.

### Supremacy Through Precedent
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes Marbury's framework allowed future justices to claim supremacy without re-litigating the power.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits Marbury with making supremacy "a living force," used in civil rights and other eras.

### Modern Implications
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees it as enabling judicial activism, where courts override democracy.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues Marbury founded supremacy by making courts the guardians of constitutional limits.
