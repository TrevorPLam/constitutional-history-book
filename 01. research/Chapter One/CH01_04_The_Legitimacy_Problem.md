# CH01: The Legitimacy Problem

## Overview
The early U.S. Supreme Court faced a fundamental legitimacy crisis: lacking explicit constitutional authority for judicial review, how could it claim and maintain public trust? This "legitimacy problem" arose from the Constitution's silence on courts invalidating laws, making assertions of power vulnerable to political backlash and accusations of overreach.

## Primary Sources
### Constitutional Silence
- **Article III**: Grants judicial power but does not authorize invalidating legislation. The Supremacy Clause (Article VI) binds judges to the Constitution but does not specify enforcement mechanisms.
- **Convention Debates**: Notes from the Philadelphia Convention show framers assumed courts could interpret laws but did not explicitly endorse nullification of acts of Congress.

### Early Court Decisions and Reactions
- **Chisholm v. Georgia (1793)**: The Court's first major assertion of federal authority over states provoked outrage. Georgia refused to appear, arguing the Court lacked jurisdiction. Newspapers and politicians labeled it an "usurpation."
- **Contemporary Newspapers**: Federalist papers defended the decision; Anti-Federalists and Jeffersonians condemned it as judicial tyranny. For example, the Virginia Gazette called it "an alarming stretch of power."
- **Virginia and Kentucky Resolutions (1798)**: Written by Jefferson and Madison, these resolutions argued states could nullify federal laws, implicitly questioning the Court's legitimacy to enforce constitutional limits without explicit text.

### Federalist Papers
- **Federalist No. 78**: Hamilton argued for judicial independence but did not claim explicit review power, leaving ambiguity that fueled legitimacy debates.

## Historian Analyses
### Crisis of Authority
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren describes the early Court as facing "hostile public opinion" due to perceived overreach. Chisholm's backlash demonstrated that judicial claims required political acceptance to gain legitimacy.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues the Court's legitimacy was contingent on public perception of its role. Without constitutional text, authority depended on "prudential judgments" and avoidance of direct confrontation.

### Building Trust Through Practice
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age of the Supreme Court" (2001), Newmyer posits legitimacy emerged from consistent, principled decisions. The Court had to demonstrate restraint and utility to earn public confidence.
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell notes Anti-Federalists viewed the Court as a potential "aristocratic" threat, undermining its democratic legitimacy. The 11th Amendment was a direct response to perceived illegitimacy.

### Comparative and Theoretical Perspectives
- **Judicial Review in State Courts**: Many states had explicit provisions, contrasting with the federal silence and highlighting the national Court's vulnerability.
- **Democratic Theory**: Historians like Gordon Wood ("The Creation of the American Republic," 1969) argue the Court's legitimacy problem stemmed from republican fears of unchecked power, requiring the judiciary to prove itself through service to the people.

## Implications
The legitimacy problem forced the Court to build authority incrementally, using cases like Marbury to establish precedent while avoiding escalation. Failure to gain trust risked institutional irrelevance or reform.
