# CH01: Long-Term Institutional Gain

## Overview
The long-term institutional gain from Marbury v. Madison (1803) was the establishment of judicial review as precedent, transforming the Supreme Court into the chief interpreter of the Constitution. Though politically defeated immediately, the decision created an enduring doctrine that elevated the judiciary's authority.

## Primary Sources
### Marbury's Immediate Legacy
- **Judicial Review Doctrine**: Marshall's opinion articulated that courts must interpret the Constitution, invalidating conflicting laws. This became the foundational precedent.
- **Subsequent Cases**: 
  - **Fletcher v. Peck (1810)**: First post-Marbury use of review, voiding a Georgia land grant as unconstitutional.
  - **McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)**: Upheld implied powers, citing Marbury.
  - **Gibbons v. Ogden (1824)**: Broadened commerce clause, reinforcing review.

### Institutional Changes
- **Court's Role**: By the 1820s, the Court was seen as the "ultimate interpreter," with review used in over 200 cases by 1860.
- **Public Acceptance**: Over time, judicial review gained legitimacy, no longer challenged as in Chisholm.

## Historian Analyses
### Establishment as Precedent
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Marbury's gain was institutional permanence. Review became "settled law," outlasting political shifts.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes the gain as embedding review in American jurisprudence, making the Court co-equal.

### From Defeat to Supremacy
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes the paradox: immediate defeat led to supremacy. Marbury's precedent allowed future assertions without backlash.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits Marbury with making review "an accepted part of our constitutional system."

### Enduring Impact
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees the gain as taming judicial power through precedent, ensuring stability.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues Marbury transformed the Court from weak to authoritative, using law to achieve political goals.
