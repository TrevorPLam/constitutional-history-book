# CH02: Strategic Case Selection: Choosing Battles the Court Can Win

## Overview
Marshall employed strategic case selection, choosing battles the Court could win to build authority incrementally. By focusing on commercial, contract, and federalism cases with clear constitutional bases, he avoided political minefields and established precedents that expanded judicial power.

## Primary Sources
### Selected Cases
- **Marbury v. Madison (1803)**: Judicial review without enforcement, winnable principle.
- **McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)**: Federal bank case, strong constitutional foundation.
- **Gibbons v. Ogden (1824)**: Commerce dispute, broad support for national markets.
- **Fletcher v. Peck (1810)**: Contracts clause, less partisan than political issues.

### Avoided Cases
- **Slavery Cases**: Postponed decisions on fugitive slave laws to avoid sectional conflict.
- **Political Appointments**: Limited challenges to executive actions.

## Historian Analyses
### Strategic Selection
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Marshall's case selection was "judicial chess," maximizing gains while minimizing risks.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes it as choosing "institutionally safe" cases to build power.

### Winable Battles
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes Marshall focused on cases with clear legal bases, avoiding ambiguity.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits selection with preventing defeats.

### Incremental Building
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees selection as enabling gradual authority expansion.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues it reflected Marshall's political acumen.
