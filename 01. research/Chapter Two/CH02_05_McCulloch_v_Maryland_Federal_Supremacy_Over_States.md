# CH02: McCulloch v. Maryland (1819): Federal Supremacy Over States

## Overview
McCulloch v. Maryland (1819) affirmed federal supremacy over states, upholding the constitutionality of the Second Bank of the United States and the doctrine of implied powers. Marshall's opinion established Congress's broad authority under the necessary and proper clause, reinforcing national over state power.

## Primary Sources
### Case Facts
- **Background**: Congress chartered the Second Bank of the United States (1816) to stabilize currency post-War of 1812. Maryland imposed a tax on the bank's Baltimore branch, claiming states could regulate federal institutions.
- **Supreme Court**: James McCulloch, bank cashier, refused to pay. Maryland courts upheld the tax; appealed to Supreme Court.

### Marshall's Opinion
- **Unanimous Decision**: 7-0 for McCulloch.
- **Key Holdings**:
  - Congress has implied powers under Article I, Section 8: "necessary and proper" allows means not explicitly listed.
  - Federal supremacy (Article VI) prevents states from taxing federal operations.
- **Famous Quote**: "Let the end be legitimate, let it be within the scope of the constitution, and all means which are appropriate, which are plainly adapted to that end, which are not prohibited, but consist with the letter and spirit of the constitution, are constitutional."

## Historian Analyses
### Federal Supremacy Established
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues McCulloch solidified national supremacy, using implied powers to counter states' rights.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes it as a cornerstone of federal authority, adapting Hamiltonian nationalism to judicial doctrine.

### Implied Powers Doctrine
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes Marshall's broad construction enabled expansive federal governance, compensating for political weakness.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits McCulloch with defining Congress's role, preventing state fragmentation.

### Impact on Federalism
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees it as balancing national integration with state limits, amid growing sectional tensions.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues McCulloch reflected Marshall's vision of a strong union, using law to achieve political goals.
