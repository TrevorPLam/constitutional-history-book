# CH02: The Constitutional Vision: Strong National Government Through Judicial Interpretation

## Overview
John Marshall's constitutional vision emphasized a strong national government achieved through broad judicial interpretation. By expanding federal powers via implied powers, commerce clause, and supremacy clause, Marshall used the Court to promote national unity and economic integration.

## Primary Sources
### Key Cases
- **Marbury v. Madison (1803)**: Established judicial review, positioning Court as interpreter.
- **McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)**: Broad "necessary and proper" interpretation for implied powers.
- **Gibbons v. Ogden (1824)**: Defined commerce clause expansively for federal regulation.

### Marshall's Writings
- **Life of George Washington (1804–1807)**: Reflected nationalist ideals, emphasizing unity.
- **Correspondence**: Letters to Joseph Story (1819) discussed building "a system of principles" for national governance.

## Historian Analyses
### Nationalist Vision
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Marshall's vision was Hamiltonian, using law to create a strong union where politics failed.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes Marshall as a "nationalist jurist," interpreting the Constitution to support federal supremacy.

### Judicial Interpretation Strategy
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes Marshall's broad construction—e.g., "necessary and proper" as flexible—to enable national governance.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits Marshall with transforming the Constitution into a living document for national purposes.

### Impact on Federalism
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees Marshall's vision as balancing state rights with national needs, amid sectional tensions.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues Marshall's interpretation reflected original intent for a strong union.
