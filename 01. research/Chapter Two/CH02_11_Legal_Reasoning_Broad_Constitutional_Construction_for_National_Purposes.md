# CH02: Legal Reasoning: Broad Constitutional Construction for National Purposes

## Overview
Marshall's legal reasoning employed broad constitutional construction to serve national purposes. By interpreting clauses like "necessary and proper" and "commerce" expansively, he created a flexible framework for federal power, enabling national governance amid sectional challenges.

## Primary Sources
### Key Reasoning Examples
- **McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)**: "Necessary" as "convenient, useful" rather than "absolutely essential," allowing implied powers.
- **Gibbons v. Ogden (1824)**: Commerce as "intercourse," including navigation, for broad federal regulation.
- **Cohens v. Virginia (1821)**: Federal review of state cases under Article III, integrating legal systems.

### Marshall's Approach
- **Textualism with Purpose**: Read text in context of national unity, not literalism.
- **Living Constitution**: Adapted to economic and social changes, like industrial growth.

## Historian Analyses
### Broad Construction Strategy
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Marshall's reasoning was purposive, using construction to achieve national goals.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes it as "nationalist jurisprudence," aligning law with economic development.

### National Purposes
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes reasoning supported market integration and political stability.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits Marshall with creating a "dynamic Constitution" for national needs.

### Impact on Federalism
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees broad construction as balancing state rights with national imperatives.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues it reflected framers' intent for a strong union.
