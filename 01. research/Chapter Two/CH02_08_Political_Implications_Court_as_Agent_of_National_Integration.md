# CH02: Political Implications: Court as Agent of National Integration

## Overview
Marshall's Court served as an agent of national integration, using judicial decisions to unify the country economically and politically. By expanding federal power over commerce and banking, the Court promoted national markets and reduced state fragmentation, aligning with early 19th-century nationalism.

## Primary Sources
### Key Cases
- **McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)**: Upheld national bank, enabling uniform currency and economic stability.
- **Gibbons v. Ogden (1824)**: Ended state monopolies, fostering interstate trade.
- **Cohens v. Virginia (1821)**: Asserted federal review of state criminal cases, integrating legal systems.

### Contemporary Reactions
- **Federalist Support**: Papers like the National Intelligencer hailed decisions as promoting unity.
- **Republican Opposition**: Critics like John Randolph warned of "centralization," but economic benefits swayed public opinion.

## Historian Analyses
### National Integration Agent
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Marshall used the Court to integrate the nation, where Congress could not due to sectionalism.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes the Court as a "nationalizing force," creating a unified legal and economic framework.

### Economic Unification
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes Marshall's decisions facilitated market integration, supporting industrial growth.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits the Court with breaking down state barriers, enabling national capitalism.

### Political Impact
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees the Court as balancing localism with nationalism, amid sectional tensions.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues Marshall's integration reflected post-War of 1812 nationalism.
