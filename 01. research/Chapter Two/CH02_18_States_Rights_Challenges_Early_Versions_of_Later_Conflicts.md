# CH02: States' Rights Challenges: Early Versions of Later Conflicts

## Overview
States' rights challenges during Marshall's era were early versions of later conflicts, foreshadowing sectional tensions over federal authority. Cases like McCulloch and Cohens tested state sovereignty, setting precedents for Civil War disputes.

## Primary Sources
### Key Challenges
- **McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)**: Maryland's tax on federal bank challenged federal supremacy.
- **Cohens v. Virginia (1821)**: Virginia resisted federal review of state criminal cases.
- **Martin v. Hunter's Lessee (1816)**: Virginia questioned appellate jurisdiction over state decisions.

### State Arguments
- **Sovereignty Claims**: States argued for retained sovereignty under the 10th Amendment.
- **Nullification Ideas**: Early versions of nullification, though not fully developed until the 1830s.

## Historian Analyses
### Early Conflict Foreshadowing
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues these challenges were "precursors" to Civil War conflicts.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes them as "testing federalism" boundaries.

### States' Rights Evolution
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes challenges grew from legal disputes to political movements.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits Marshall with delaying sectional war through judicial resolution.

### Sectional Tensions
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees challenges as reflecting North-South divides over federal power.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues they revealed constitutional ambiguities on federalism.
