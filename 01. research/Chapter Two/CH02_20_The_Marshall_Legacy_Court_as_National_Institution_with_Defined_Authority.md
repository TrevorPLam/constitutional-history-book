# CH02: The Marshall Legacy: Court as National Institution with Defined Authority

## Overview
Marshall's legacy was transforming the Court into a national institution with defined authority, balancing power with restraint. By 1835, the Supreme Court was co-equal, respected, and integral to American governance, with clear boundaries.

## Primary Sources
### Legacy Achievements
- **Judicial Review**: Established in Marbury, used in over 200 cases by 1860.
- **Federal Supremacy**: McCulloch and Gibbons defined federal power.
- **Institutional Practices**: Unanimous opinions, precedential system, and procedures.

### Defined Authority
- **Scope**: Clear limits on judicial power, avoiding overreach.
- **Legitimacy**: Public respect through reason and consistency.
- **Continuity**: Framework for future courts (e.g., Taney Court).

## Historian Analyses
### National Institution
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Marshall created a "national judiciary" essential to governance.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes the Court as a "central national institution" by 1835.

### Defined Authority
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes Marshall balanced authority with defined limits, preventing tyranny.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits Marshall with establishing the Court's constitutional role.

### Enduring Legacy
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees the legacy as ensuring judicial independence within democracy.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues it fulfilled framers' vision for a co-equal judiciary.
