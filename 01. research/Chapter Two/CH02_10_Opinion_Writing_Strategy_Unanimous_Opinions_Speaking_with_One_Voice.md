# CH02: Opinion-Writing Strategy: Unanimous Opinions Speaking with One Voice

## Overview
Marshall employed an opinion-writing strategy of unanimous opinions to project institutional unity and authority. By speaking with one voice, the Court presented a cohesive front, enhancing legitimacy and reducing partisan vulnerability.

## Primary Sources
### Unanimous Decisions
- **Marbury v. Madison (1803)**: 4-0 unanimous, establishing review.
- **McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)**: 7-0 unanimous, upholding implied powers.
- **Gibbons v. Ogden (1824)**: 6-0 unanimous, defining commerce clause.
- **Overall Record**: Marshall issued 1,042 unanimous decisions during his tenure.

### Marshall's Method
- **Single Author**: Marshall wrote most opinions himself, ensuring consistency.
- **Consultation**: He consulted justices privately to achieve consensus, avoiding public dissents.
- **Language**: Used clear, accessible prose to build public understanding.

## Historian Analyses
### Unity Strategy
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues unanimity built institutional strength, presenting the Court as non-partisan.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes unanimity as a political tool to gain acceptance.

### Legitimacy Building
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes unanimity enhanced public respect, making decisions appear reasoned rather than political.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits Marshall with creating a "judicial voice" through unified opinions.

### Long-Term Impact
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees unanimity as masking Federalist bias while building authority.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues it established the Court as a deliberative body.
