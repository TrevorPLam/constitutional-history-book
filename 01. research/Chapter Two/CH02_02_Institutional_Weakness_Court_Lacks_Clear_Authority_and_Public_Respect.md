# CH02: Institutional Weakness: Court Still Lacks Clear Authority and Public Respect

## Overview
Despite Marbury v. Madison (1803), the early Marshall Court remained institutionally weak, lacking clear constitutional authority and public respect. Republicans viewed it as a Federalist stronghold, with threats of impeachment, jurisdiction limits, and minimal enforcement power undermining its legitimacy.

## Primary Sources
### Judicial Threats and Reforms
- **Repeal of Judiciary Act (1802)**: Congress eliminated the Court's August term and circuit reforms, leaving justices without sessions from April 1802 to February 1803. This disrupted operations and signaled political hostility.
- **Impeachment Attempts**: In 1804, Republicans impeached Justice Samuel Chase, accusing him of bias. Though acquitted, it intimidated the Court.
- **Jefferson's Views**: Jefferson wrote in 1801 that Federalists "retreated into the judiciary as a stronghold," calling justices "secure for life and skulking their responsibilities."

### Operational Weaknesses
- **Circuit Duties**: Justices still rode circuits, traveling extensively and limiting Supreme Court focus. No permanent building or staff.
- **Low Caseload**: Few cases appealed; docket remained small, reducing visibility.
- **Enforcement Issues**: No executive power to enforce decisions; relied on voluntary compliance.

### Public Perception
- **Newspaper Coverage**: Republican press criticized the Court as elitist. Federalist papers defended it but admitted vulnerability.
- **Political Rhetoric**: Kentucky Republican John Randolph called the Court "an old woman" in 1806, reflecting low esteem.

## Historian Analyses
### Post-Marbury Vulnerability
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues the Court was "fragile" despite Marbury, facing Republican dominance and lacking enforcement mechanisms.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White notes institutional weakness: no clear authority, public skepticism, and political threats delayed supremacy building.

### Lack of Respect
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren describes the Court as "impotent" in the early 1800s, overshadowed by elected branches and viewed as partisan.
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson sees public respect growing slowly; early cases like Marbury faced ridicule rather than reverence.

### Contextual Pressures
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell highlights Anti-Federalist distrust, with Republicans seeing the Court as aristocratic.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues weakness stemmed from ambiguous jurisdiction and political hostility, forcing strategic adaptation.
