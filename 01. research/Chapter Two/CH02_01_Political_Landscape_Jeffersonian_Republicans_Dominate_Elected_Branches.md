# CH02: Political Landscape: Jeffersonian Republicans Dominate Elected Branches

## Overview
From 1800 to 1824, Jeffersonian Republicans dominated the elected branches of the U.S. government, controlling the presidency, Congress, and state governments. This one-party dominance, known as the "Era of Good Feelings" after 1816, shaped the political context in which John Marshall built judicial supremacy, forcing the Court to navigate a hostile environment.

## Primary Sources
### Presidential Elections
- **Election of 1800**: Jefferson defeats Adams, Republicans gain control. Jefferson serves 1801–1809, followed by Madison (1809–1817), Monroe (1817–1825).
- **Election of 1804**: Jefferson reelected overwhelmingly, Republicans solidify power.
- **Election of 1816**: Monroe wins unopposed, marking peak of one-party rule.
- **Election of 1820**: Monroe reelected unanimously in electoral college.

### Congressional Composition
- **House of Representatives**: Republicans held majority throughout (1801–1825), often with supermajorities.
- **Senate**: Republicans controlled after 1801, with brief Federalist holds in New England states.
- **Key Legislation**: Embargo Act (1807), War of 1812, Missouri Compromise (1820) reflect Republican dominance.

### State Governments
- **Dominance**: Republicans controlled most state legislatures and governorships, implementing Jeffersonian policies like agrarian reforms and limited federal power.
- **Federalist Strongholds**: New England states retained Federalist influence, but nationally Republicans prevailed.

## Historian Analyses
### Era of Good Feelings
- **One-Party Rule**: Historians like George Dangerfield ("The Era of Good Feelings," 1952) describe 1817–1825 as a period of minimal partisan conflict, with Republicans unchallenged.
- **Republican Ideology**: Jeffersonian democracy emphasized states' rights, agrarianism, and opposition to Federalist elitism, dominating policy.

### Impact on Judiciary
- **Hostile Environment**: Republicans viewed the Court as Federalist bastion. Jefferson's attempts to impeach Justice Samuel Chase (1804) reflected tensions.
- **Marshall's Challenge**: As historian R. Kent Newmyer ("John Marshall and the Heroic Age," 2001) notes, Marshall built Court power amid Republican hostility, using legal strategy to compensate.

### Broader Context
- **Post-War Unity**: War of 1812 fostered nationalism, but Republicans maintained control.
- **Transition to Democracy**: By 1820s, internal Republican divisions emerged, leading to Democratic Party formation.
