# CH02: Gibbons v. Ogden (1824): Federal Control Over Interstate Commerce

## Overview
Gibbons v. Ogden (1824) established federal control over interstate commerce, defining Congress's power under the Commerce Clause. Marshall's opinion invalidated state-granted monopolies on navigation, reinforcing national economic integration and federal supremacy.

## Primary Sources
### Case Facts
- **Background**: New York granted Aaron Ogden exclusive rights to operate steamboats between New York and New Jersey. Thomas Gibbons, with a federal coasting license, competed, leading to a lawsuit.
- **Supreme Court**: Gibbons appealed after New York courts upheld Ogden's monopoly, arguing federal license preempted state law.

### Marshall's Opinion
- **Unanimous Decision**: 6-0 for Gibbons.
- **Key Holdings**:
  - Commerce includes navigation, and Congress regulates interstate commerce under Article I, Section 8.
  - Federal law (coasting license) preempted state monopoly, as commerce is national.
- **Famous Quote**: "The power to regulate commerce... is complete in itself, may be exercised to its utmost extent, and acknowledges no limitations, other than are prescribed in the Constitution."

## Historian Analyses
### Federal Commerce Authority
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Gibbons cemented federal economic power, using commerce clause to unify markets.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes it as expanding federal reach, supporting national capitalism.

### National Integration
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes Gibbons promoted economic unity, countering state fragmentation.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits it with defining federal role in commerce, enabling later regulations.

### Impact on Federalism
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees it as balancing state rights with national needs, amid industrial growth.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues Gibbons reflected Marshall's vision of a connected nation.
