# CH02: Democratic Resistance: Growing Concern Over Judicial Power

## Overview
Democratic resistance grew as Marshall's Court expanded judicial power, sparking concerns over judicial supremacy. Republicans and states' rights advocates criticized the Court for overreaching, leading to political backlash that tested Marshall's balance.

## Primary Sources
### Resistance Sources
- **Congressional Debates**: 1820s discussions on limiting Court jurisdiction, especially after McCulloch.
- **State Resolutions**: Virginia and Kentucky legislatures passed resolutions condemning judicial overreach.
- **Newspaper Criticism**: Republican papers like the Richmond Enquirer attacked decisions as "usurpation."

### Key Criticisms
- **Judicial Usurpation**: Claims Court exceeded constitutional authority.
- **Anti-Federalist Revival**: Echoed 1790s fears of centralized power.
- **States' Rights**: Emphasis on state sovereignty against federal judicial dominance.

## Historian Analyses
### Growing Resistance
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues resistance intensified as Court's power became evident, peaking in the 1820s.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes resistance as part of the "democratic challenge" to judicial authority.

### Democratic Concerns
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes resistance reflected fears of unelected judges overriding elected branches.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits resistance with keeping Court in check.

### Backlash Cycle
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees resistance as a backlash cycle, mirroring Chisholm's aftermath.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues it stemmed from democratic ideals opposing elitist control.
