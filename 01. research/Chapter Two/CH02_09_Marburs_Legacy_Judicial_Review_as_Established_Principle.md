# CH02: Marbury's Legacy: Judicial Review as Established Principle

## Overview
Marbury v. Madison (1803) established judicial review as an enduring principle, transforming the Supreme Court into the ultimate interpreter of the Constitution. Marshall's opinion created the foundation for judicial supremacy, enabling future courts to assert authority over elected branches.

## Primary Sources
### Marbury v. Madison (1803)
- **Decision**: Declared Judiciary Act of 1789 unconstitutional, establishing review.
- **Key Quote**: "It is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is."
- **Strategic Element**: Avoided enforcement against Jefferson, focusing on principle.

### Subsequent Uses
- **Fletcher v. Peck (1810)**: First post-Marbury use, voiding Georgia land grant.
- **Dartmouth College v. Woodward (1819)**: Protected contracts from state interference.
- **Cohens v. Virginia (1821)**: Extended review to state criminal cases.

## Historian Analyses
### Established Principle
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Marbury's legacy was creating a "settled principle" of review, used in over 200 cases by 1860.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes it as the cornerstone, making the Court co-equal.

### Enduring Impact
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes Marbury's framework allowed future assertions without re-litigating the power.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren credits Marbury with making review "an accepted part of our constitutional system."

### Institutional Legacy
- **Saul Cornell**: In "The Other Founders" (1999), Cornell sees Marbury's legacy as enabling judicial activism while providing stability.
- **Jack Rakove**: In "Original Meanings" (1996), Rakove argues it transformed the Court from weak to authoritative.
