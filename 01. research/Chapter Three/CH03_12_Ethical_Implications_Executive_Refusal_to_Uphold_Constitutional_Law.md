# CH03: Ethical Implications: Executive Refusal to Uphold Constitutional Law

## Overview
Jackson's refusal to uphold constitutional law raised profound ethical implications for American democracy. The executive's defiance established a dangerous precedent that constitutional obligations could be ignored when politically inconvenient.

## Primary Sources
### Constitutional Violations
- **Oath of Office**: Jackson swore to "preserve, protect and defend the Constitution" but ignored Court decisions.
- **Treaty Obligations**: U.S. government violated numerous treaties with Cherokee Nation despite constitutional supremacy.
- **Rule of Law**: Executive actions demonstrated that law applied only when politically acceptable.

### Moral Consequences
- **Trail of Tears**: Forced removal resulted in 4,000+ Cherokee deaths, demonstrating human cost of constitutional defiance.
- **Property Rights**: Cherokee lands seized illegally despite constitutional protections.
- **Legal Precedent**: Executive defiance established template for future constitutional violations.

## Historian Analyses
### Ethical Crisis
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues Jackson's actions represented "ethical breakdown" in constitutional government.
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer describes the refusal as "moral failure" of executive leadership.
- **Richard E. Ellis**: In "The Union at Risk" (1987), Ellis analyzes the ethical implications as "dangerous precedent" for constitutional violation.

### Constitutional Morality
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Course of American Democracy" (1984), Remini acknowledges the moral cost but defends Jackson's democratic mandate.
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesinger criticizes the ethical implications as "corruption of constitutional principle."
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe emphasizes the ethical dimensions as "tragedy of American democracy."

### Historical Consequences
- **Francis Paul Prucha**: In "The Great Father" (1984), Prucha analyzes the ethical implications within broader pattern of American Indian policy.
- **Jill Lepore**: In "The Name of War" (1998), Lepore places Jackson's ethical failure in longer history of American constitutional violations.
- **Sean Wilentz**: In "The Rise of American Democracy" (2005), Wilentz argues the ethical implications revealed "dark side" of Jacksonian democracy.
