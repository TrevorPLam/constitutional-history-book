# CH03: The Constitutional Question: Who Decides the Meaning of the Constitution?

## Overview
The Jacksonian era raised the fundamental constitutional question of who decides the Constitution's meaning. Jackson's challenge to Marshall's judicial supremacy created a crisis of constitutional interpretation, testing whether the president, Congress, or Court had final authority.

## Primary Sources
### Jackson's Position
- **Bank Veto Message (1832)**: "The Congress, the Executive, and the Court must each for itself be guided by its own opinion of the Constitution."
- **Proclamation on Nullification (1832)**: Asserted presidential authority to interpret Constitution independently.
- **Correspondence**: Jackson's letters emphasizing executive responsibility to the people over judicial opinions.

### Marshall's Response
- **Opinions**: Consistent assertion of judicial supremacy in constitutional interpretation.
- **Marbury v. Madison (1803)**: "It is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is."
- **Private Letters**: Marshall's defense of judicial independence and constitutional guardianship.

## Historian Analyses
### Constitutional Authority Crisis
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues this was the first major challenge to judicial supremacy since Marbury.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes it as a "constitutional crisis" over interpretation authority.
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Course of American Democracy" (1984), Remini emphasizes Jackson's democratic theory of constitutional interpretation.

### Democratic vs. Judicial Interpretation
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesinger argues Jackson represented "popular constitutionalism" against judicial elitism.
- **Richard Hofstadter**: In "The American Political Tradition" (1948), Hofstadter analyzes the conflict as between "democratic interpretation" and "judicial restraint."
- **Larry Kramer**: In "The People Themselves" (2004), Kramer traces Jackson's challenge to broader populist constitutional theory.

### Long-Term Implications
- **Keith Whittington**: In "Constitutional Interpretation" (1999), Whittington notes Jackson established precedent for executive constitutional defiance.
- **Mark Tushnet**: In "Taking the Constitution Away from the Courts" (1999), Tushnet places Jackson in tradition of popular constitutionalism.
