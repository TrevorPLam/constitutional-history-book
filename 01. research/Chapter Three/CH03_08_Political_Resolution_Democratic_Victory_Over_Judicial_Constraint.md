# CH03: Political Resolution: Democratic Victory Over Judicial Constraint

## Overview
The Bank War ended with a clear political resolution: democratic victory over judicial constraint. Jackson's triumph demonstrated that popular support could overcome judicial authority, establishing a new balance of power between the Court and elected branches.

## Primary Sources
### Democratic Victory
- **1832 Election**: Jackson's landslide reelection (219-49 electoral votes) endorsed bank opposition.
- **Bank's Demise**: National bank charter expired 1836, never rechartered during Jackson's presidency.
- **Pet Banks**: State banks received federal deposits, creating new banking system.

### Judicial Defeat
- **Marshall's Silence**: No major Court decisions challenging Jackson's actions.
- **Institutional Weakness**: Court unable to enforce its constitutional interpretations.
- **Political Reality**: Judicial supremacy proved dependent on executive compliance.

## Historian Analyses
### Democratic Triumph
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesinger argues the Bank War represented "triumph of popular democracy over aristocratic privilege."
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Course of American Democracy" (1984), Remini describes it as "victory for the common man."
- **Charles Sellers**: In "The Market Revolution" (1991), Sellers analyzes the victory as "resistance to capitalist consolidation."

### Judicial Constraint
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues the resolution demonstrated "limits of judicial power without political support."
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer describes it as "defeat for judicial supremacy."
- **Richard E. Ellis**: In "The Union at Risk" (1987), Ellis emphasizes the resolution established "executive supremacy over judicial constraint."

### Constitutional Implications
- **Sean Wilentz**: In "The Rise of American Democracy" (2005), Wilentz argues the resolution created "new constitutional order" with stronger executive.
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe describes the victory as "triumph of presidential democracy."
- **Keith Whittington**: In "Constitutional Interpretation" (1999), Whittington notes the resolution established "precedent for executive defiance of judicial authority."
