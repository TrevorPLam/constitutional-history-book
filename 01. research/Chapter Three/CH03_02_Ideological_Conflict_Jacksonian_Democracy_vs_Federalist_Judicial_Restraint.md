# CH03: Ideological Conflict: Jacksonian Democracy vs. Federalist Judicial Restraint

## Overview
The Jacksonian era featured an intense ideological conflict between popular democracy and Federalist judicial restraint. Jackson's majoritarian vision directly challenged the Marshall Court's elitist, nationalist constitutional interpretation, creating a fundamental clash over who should govern in America.

## Primary Sources
### Jacksonian Ideology
- **Jackson's Bank Veto (1832)**: Argued the bank benefited "the rich and powerful" at the expense of "the farmers, mechanics, and laborers."
- **Proclamation to the People (1832)**: Asserted presidential independence from congressional and judicial constraints.
- **Correspondence**: Letters emphasizing the president as "direct representative" of the people.

### Federalist Judicial Philosophy
- **Marshall's Opinions**: Consistent emphasis on national supremacy, property rights, and judicial review.
- **Federalist Papers**: Hamilton's vision of strong central government and judicial independence.
- **Story's Commentaries**: Joseph Story's defense of judicial power and constitutional nationalism.

## Historian Analyses
### Ideological Clash
- **Marvin Meyers**: In "The Jacksonian Persuasion" (1957), Meyers argues Jacksonian democracy represented a "new political consciousness" challenging elite rule.
- **Richard Hofstadter**: In "The American Political Tradition" (1948), Hofstadter describes the conflict as between "democratic capitalism" and "aristocratic constitutionalism."
- **Lee Benson**: In "The Concept of Jacksonian Democracy" (1961), Benson emphasizes the class-based nature of the ideological conflict.

### Judicial vs. Democratic Values
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White contrasts the Court's "legal rationalism" with Jackson's "political populism."
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Marshall represented "constitutionalism" against Jackson's "majoritarianism."
- **John William Ward**: In "Andrew Jackson: Symbol for an Age" (1955), Ward analyzes Jackson as a symbol of democratic revolt against elite control.
