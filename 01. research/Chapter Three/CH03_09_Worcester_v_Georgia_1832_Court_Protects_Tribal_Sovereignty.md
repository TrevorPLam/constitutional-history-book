# CH03: Worcester v. Georgia (1832): Court Protects Tribal Sovereignty

## Overview
Worcester v. Georgia (1832) represented the Marshall Court's final assertion of judicial supremacy, protecting Cherokee tribal sovereignty against state encroachment. The decision demonstrated the Court's willingness to defend constitutional rights even when politically unpopular.

## Primary Sources
### Worcester v. Georgia (1832)
- **Decision**: 6-1 majority holding Georgia laws violated federal treaties with Cherokee Nation.
- **Marshall's Opinion**: "The Cherokee Nation... is a distinct community, occupying its own territory."
- **Legal Reasoning**: Federal treaties supreme over state laws under Constitution.

### Cherokee Cases Context
- **Cherokee Nation v. Georgia (1831)**: Marshall ruled tribe was "domestic dependent nation" but lacked standing.
- **Jackson's Removal Policy**: Indian Removal Act (1830) authorized forced relocation of eastern tribes.
- **Treaty Violations**: Georgia ignored federal treaties protecting Cherokee lands.

## Historian Analyses
### Tribal Sovereignty Defense
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues Worcester was Marshall's "last great stand for constitutional principle."
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes the decision as "moral high point" of Marshall Court.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren emphasizes Worcester as "great defense of minority rights."

### Constitutional Courage
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Course of American Democracy" (1984), Remini notes Marshall's "courageous defense" of Cherokee rights.
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesiger analyzes Worcester as "judicial resistance to popular democracy."
- **Francis Paul Prucha**: In "The Great Father" (1984), Prucha emphasizes Worcester as "landmark in federal Indian law."

### Political Context
- **Michael F. Holt**: In "The Rise and Fall of the American Whig Party" (1999), Holt places Worcester in broader sectional conflict.
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe describes Worcester as "clash between constitutionalism and expansion."
- **Jill Lepore**: In "The Name of War" (1998), Lepore analyzes Worcester within American Indian legal history.
