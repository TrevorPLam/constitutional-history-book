# CH03: Institutional Context: Court as Bastion of Elite, National Authority

## Overview
During the Jacksonian era, the Supreme Court stood as a bastion of elite, national authority against rising democratic forces. The Court represented the institutional embodiment of Federalist principles—national supremacy, property rights, and judicial independence—making it a natural target for Jacksonian populism.

## Primary Sources
### Court Composition
- **Marshall Court Justices**: Predominantly Federalist/National Republican appointees, representing elite legal establishment.
- **Geographic Distribution**: Justices from commercial, urban centers rather than western/frontier regions.
- **Social Background**: Educated, wealthy, connected to commercial interests.

### Institutional Characteristics
- **Procedural Formality**: Strict rules, Latin maxims, complex legal reasoning inaccessible to common citizens.
- **National Perspective**: Consistent emphasis on federal over state power, commerce over agriculture.
- **Property Rights Focus**: Protection of contracts, corporations, and economic elites.

## Historian Analyses
### Elite Institution
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes the Court as "the last bastion of Federalist nationalism" in the Jacksonian era.
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues the Court represented "elite constitutionalism" against democratic majoritarianism.
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren emphasizes the Court's role as "guardian of property and commerce."

### National Authority
- **Herbert Johnson**: In "John Marshall" (1998), Johnson notes the Court's "nationalist vision" conflicted with Jackson's states' rights approach.
- **John Marshall**: In his own opinions, consistently advocated for strong federal government and national economic integration.
- **Joseph Story**: In "Commentaries on the Constitution" (1833), defended judicial power as essential to national unity.

### Democratic Opposition
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesinger portrays the Court as "aristocratic obstacle" to democratic progress.
- **Richard Hofstadter**: In "The American Political Tradition" (1948), Hofstadter analyzes the Court as "elitist counterweight" to popular democracy.
