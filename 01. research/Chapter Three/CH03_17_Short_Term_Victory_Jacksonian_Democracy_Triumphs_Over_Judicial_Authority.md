# CH03: Short-Term Victory: Jacksonian Democracy Triumphs Over Judicial Authority

## Overview
Jacksonian democracy achieved short-term victory over judicial authority, demonstrating that popular support could overcome constitutional constraints. The Jackson era marked the high point of democratic challenge to judicial supremacy.

## Primary Sources
### Democratic Triumph
- **1832 Election**: Jackson's landslide victory endorsed bank opposition and executive defiance.
- **Bank's Destruction**: National bank ended 1836, replaced by state banking system.
- **Indian Removal**: Worcester decision ignored, Trail of Tears proceeded 1838.

### Judicial Defeat
- **Limited Enforcement**: Court decisions ignored when politically inconvenient.
- **Marshall's Retreat**: Reduced constitutional challenges after 1832.
- **Institutional Weakness**: Court unable to enforce its interpretations.

## Historian Analyses
### Democratic Victory
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesinger argues the era represented "triumph of popular democracy over constitutional restraint."
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Course of American Democracy" (1984), Remini describes it as "victory for the common man."
- **Charles Sellers**: In "The Market Revolution" (1991), Sellers analyzes the victory as "resistance to elite constitutionalism."

### Judicial Defeat
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues the era demonstrated "limits of judicial power without political support."
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer describes it as "defeat for judicial supremacy."
- **Richard E. Ellis**: In "The Union at Risk" (1987), Ellis emphasizes the victory as "constitutional revolution" favoring executive power.

### Political Implications
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe analyzes the victory as "triumph of presidential democracy."
- **Sean Wilentz**: In "The Rise of American Democracy" (2005), Wilentz emphasizes the victory as "defining moment" in executive-judicial relations.
- **Michael F. Holt**: In "The Rise and Fall of the American Whig Party" (1999), Holt places the victory in broader political realignment.
