# CH03: The Enforcement Problem: Court Lacks Power to Implement Decisions

## Overview
The Jacksonian era revealed the fundamental enforcement problem: the Supreme Court lacks power to implement its decisions. Without executive cooperation, judicial rulings remain theoretical rather than real, demonstrating the limits of judicial supremacy.

## Primary Sources
### Enforcement Mechanisms
- **Constitutional Design**: No enforcement power granted to judiciary in Article III.
- **Marbury v. Madison (1803)**: Court declared laws unconstitutional but relied on executive compliance.
- **Worcester v. Georgia (1832)**: Decision ignored by Jackson administration and Georgia state government.

### Historical Examples
- **Cherokee Cases**: Court protected tribal rights, but removal proceeded anyway.
- **Bank War**: Court upheld bank constitutionality, but Jackson destroyed it.
- **State Defiance**: Multiple states ignored Court decisions with federal support.

## Historian Analyses
### Enforcement Limitations
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues the enforcement problem revealed "structural weakness" of judicial power.
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer emphasizes that judicial supremacy requires "political acquiescence."
- **Richard E. Ellis**: In "The Union at Risk" (1987), Ellis analyzes the enforcement problem as "constitutional design flaw."

### Political Dependence
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Course of American Democracy" (1984), Remini argues Jackson demonstrated that "executive power trumps judicial authority."
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesinger describes the enforcement problem as "fundamental challenge to judicial supremacy."
- **Keith Whittington**: In "Constitutional Interpretation" (1999), Whittington emphasizes that judicial power depends on "institutional cooperation."

### Constitutional Implications
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe analyzes the enforcement problem as revealing "limits of constitutional government."
- **Sean Wilentz**: In "The Rise of American Democracy" (2005), Wilentz argues the enforcement problem established "precedent for executive defiance."
- **Mark Tushnet**: In "Taking the Constitution Away from the Courts" (1999), Tushnet places the enforcement problem in broader context of popular constitutionalism.
