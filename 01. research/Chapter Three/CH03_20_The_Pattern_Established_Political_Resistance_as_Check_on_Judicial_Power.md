# CH03: The Pattern Established: Political Resistance as Check on Judicial Power

## Overview
The Jacksonian challenge established a lasting pattern: political resistance serves as a check on judicial power. This template for democratic defiance would recur throughout American history whenever courts overstepped political boundaries.

## Primary Sources
### Resistance Template
- **Executive Defiance**: Jackson's successful resistance to Court decisions.
- **Popular Support**: Democratic endorsement of executive challenges to judicial authority.
- **Political Legitimacy**: Elected branches' claim to represent popular will over judicial interpretation.

### Recurring Pattern
- **Constitutional Crisis**: When judicial decisions conflict with popular democracy.
- **Political Resolution**: Executive and legislative resistance to judicial overreach.
- **Institutional Adaptation**: Court retreat to preserve institutional relevance.

## Historian Analyses
### Pattern Establishment
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues the Jacksonian era "established the pattern" for political resistance to judicial power.
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer emphasizes the era as "template for democratic defiance" of judicial authority.
- **Richard E. Ellis**: In "The Union at Risk" (1987), Ellis analyzes the pattern as "recurrent feature" of American constitutional politics.

### Democratic Check
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesinger argues the pattern represents "democratic check" on judicial supremacy.
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Course of American Democracy" (1984), Remini emphasizes the pattern as "balance between judicial and popular authority."
- **Keith Whittington**: In "Constitutional Interpretation" (1999), Whittington analyzes the pattern as "institutional constraint" on judicial power.

### Historical Recurrence
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe argues the pattern would "recur throughout American history."
- **Sean Wilentz**: In "The Rise of American Democracy" (2005), Wilentz emphasizes the pattern as "enduring feature" of American constitutionalism.
- **Mark Tushnet**: In "Taking the Constitution Away from the Courts" (1999), Tushnet places the pattern in broader context of popular constitutionalism.
