# CH03: Jackson's Veto: Political Rejection of Constitutional Interpretation

## Overview
Jackson's 1832 bank veto represented a direct political rejection of Marshall's constitutional interpretation. By asserting the president's independent authority to interpret the Constitution, Jackson challenged the very foundation of judicial supremacy established in Marbury v. Madison.

## Primary Sources
### Veto Message (July 10, 1832)
- **Constitutional Argument**: "Each public officer must interpret the Constitution for himself."
- **Democratic Theory**: President as "direct representative of the American people."
- **Rejection of Precedent**: Dismissed McCulloch v. Maryland as judicial overreach.

### Political Context
- **Election Year**: Veto issued during 1832 presidential campaign.
- **Popular Appeal**: Framed as struggle between "common man" and "privileged elite."
- **Congressional Support**: House sustained veto, Senate upheld by Vice President Calhoun's tie-breaking vote.

## Historian Analyses
### Constitutional Rejection
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Course of American Democracy" (1984), Remini argues the veto represented "Jacksonian constitutional theory" of popular interpretation.
- **Richard E. Ellis**: In "The Union at Risk" (1987), Ellis analyzes the veto as "unprecedented assertion of executive power."
- **Michael F. Holt**: In "The Rise and Fall of the American Whig Party" (1999), Holt emphasizes the veto's political brilliance.

### Political Strategy
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesinger describes the veto as "masterstroke of democratic politics."
- **Charles Sellers**: In "The Market Revolution" (1991), Sellers analyzes the veto as resistance to capitalist transformation.
- **Sean Wilentz**: In "The Rise of American Democracy" (2005), Wilentz emphasizes the veto's populist appeal.

### Constitutional Crisis
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues the veto created "constitutional crisis" over interpretation authority.
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer describes it as "direct assault" on judicial supremacy.
- **Keith Whittington**: In "Constitutional Interpretation" (1999), Whittington notes the veto established precedent for executive constitutional defiance.
