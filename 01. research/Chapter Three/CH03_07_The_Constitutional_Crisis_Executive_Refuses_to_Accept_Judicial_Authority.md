# CH03: The Constitutional Crisis: Executive Refuses to Accept Judicial Authority

## Overview
Jackson's removal of bank deposits in 1833 created a constitutional crisis when the executive branch refused to accept judicial authority. This defiance demonstrated that judicial supremacy depended on political acquiescence, not constitutional reasoning alone.

## Primary Sources
### Removal of Deposits (October 1833)
- **Executive Action**: Jackson ordered Treasury Secretary Roger Taney to remove federal deposits from Bank of the United States.
- **Defiance of Congress**: Action taken despite Senate's rejection of Taney's nomination as Treasury Secretary.
- **Constitutional Claim**: Jackson asserted executive authority independent of judicial and congressional constraints.

### Congressional Response
- **Senate Censure (March 28, 1834)**: Formal rebuke of Jackson for assuming "authority and power not conferred by the Constitution."
- **Censure Text**: Accused Jackson of violating separation of powers and exceeding executive authority.
- **Political Debate**: Henry Clay led opposition, arguing Jackson had become "above the law."

## Historian Analyses
### Constitutional Crisis
- **Richard E. Ellis**: In "The Union at Risk" (1987), Ellis argues the removal created "the most serious constitutional crisis since 1800."
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Course of American Democracy" (1984), Remini describes it as "bold assertion of presidential power."
- **Michael F. Holt**: In "The Rise and Fall of the American Whig Party" (1999), Holt analyzes the crisis as "defining moment" in executive-legislative relations.

### Executive Defiance
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesinger portrays Jackson as "president as tribune" defying institutional constraints.
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White emphasizes the crisis revealed "limits of judicial power."
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues it showed "judicial supremacy required political support."

### Political Consequences
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe describes the crisis as "victory for presidential democracy."
- **Sean Wilentz**: In "The Rise of American Democracy" (2005), Wilentz emphasizes the crisis established "precedent for executive defiance."
- **John M. McFaul**: In "The Bank War" (1967), McFaul analyzes the crisis as "constitutional revolution."
