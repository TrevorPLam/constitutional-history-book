# CH03: McCulloch's Legacy: National Bank as Federal Power Symbol

## Overview
The national bank, upheld in McCulloch v. Maryland (1819), became the symbol of federal power that Jackson sought to destroy. The Bank War represented the ultimate confrontation between Marshall's nationalist vision and Jackson's states' rights populism.

## Primary Sources
### McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)
- **Decision**: Upheld bank constitutionality under "necessary and proper" clause.
- **Marshall's Reasoning**: Broad interpretation of federal power, national supremacy.
- **Symbolic Importance**: Bank as embodiment of national economic integration.

### Jackson's Bank War
- **Veto Message (1832)**: Rejected bank renewal, arguing it benefited elites.
- **Removal of Deposits (1833)**: Transferred funds to state banks, defying Congress.
- **Censure Resolution (1834)**: Senate's formal rebuke of Jackson's actions.

## Historian Analyses
### Bank as Federal Power Symbol
- **Charles Warren**: In "The Supreme Court in United States History" (1922), Warren emphasizes McCulloch as "the great case of federal supremacy."
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer argues the bank represented "Marshall's nationalist vision."
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White describes the bank as "symbol of national economic integration."

### Jackson's Opposition
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Bank War" (1967), Remini portrays Jackson as champion of the common man against financial monopoly.
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesiger analyzes the bank as "aristocratic institution" threatening democracy.
- **John M. McFaul**: In "The Bank War" (1967), McFaul emphasizes the constitutional and political dimensions of Jackson's opposition.

### Constitutional Crisis
- **Richard E. Ellis**: In "The Union at Risk" (1987), Ellis analyzes the Bank War as constitutional crisis over executive power.
- **Michael F. Holt**: In "The Rise and Fall of the American Whig Party" (1999), Holt places the Bank War in broader political context.
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe contrasts Jackson's populism with Whig nationalism.
