# CH03: Jackson's Response: "John Marshall Has Made His Decision, Now Let Him Enforce It"

## Overview
Jackson's famous response to Worcester v. Georgia—"John Marshall has made his decision, now let him enforce it"—represented the ultimate executive defiance of judicial authority. This quote encapsulated the Jacksonian challenge to judicial supremacy.

## Primary Sources
### Jackson's Statement
- **Attributed Quote**: "John Marshall has made his decision, now let him enforce it."
- **Historical Context**: Private remark reported by Congressman John B. Clark.
- **Public Policy**: Jackson continued Indian removal despite Court decision.

### Executive Action
- **Treaty Negotiations**: Jackson negotiated New Echota Treaty (1835) with Cherokee faction.
- **Military Enforcement**: Used U.S. Army to enforce removal, beginning Trail of Tears (1838).
- **State Compliance**: Georgia continued to violate Cherokee rights with federal support.

## Historian Analyses
### Executive Defiance
- **Robert Remini**: In "Andrew Jackson and the Course of American Democracy" (1984), Remini argues the quote showed Jackson's "complete rejection of judicial authority."
- **Richard E. Ellis**: In "The Union at Risk" (1987), Ellis describes it as "most famous act of executive defiance in American history."
- **Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**: In "The Age of Jackson" (1945), Schlesinger analyzes the quote as "triumph of popular will over constitutional principle."

### Constitutional Crisis
- **G. Edward White**: In "The Marshall Court and Cultural Change" (1987), White argues the response demonstrated "complete breakdown of judicial authority."
- **R. Kent Newmyer**: In "John Marshall and the Heroic Age" (2001), Newmyer describes it as "final defeat for Marshall's constitutional vision."
- **Michael F. Holt**: In "The Rise and Fall of the American Whig Party" (1999), Holt emphasizes the response as "constitutional revolution."

### Historical Significance
- **Daniel Walker Howe**: In "What Hath God Wrought" (2007), Howe analyzes the quote as "defining moment in executive-judicial relations."
- **Sean Wilentz**: In "The Rise of American Democracy" (2005), Wilentz emphasizes the response as "precedent for executive defiance."
- **Francis Paul Prucha**: In "The Great Father" (1984), Prucha places the response in context of American Indian policy.
